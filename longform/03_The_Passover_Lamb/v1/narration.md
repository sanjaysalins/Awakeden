# The Passover Lamb — long-form narration (DRAFT v1)

**Series:** types-shadows (#1) · **Format:** 16:9, ~6–8 min, 7-movement spine
**Core text:** Exodus 12 · **NT fulfillment:** 1 Corinthians 5:7 · John 19:33–36 · 1 Peter 1:18–19
**Thread (one spine, hook→landing):** The Passover instructions are strangely over-specified —
a lamb without blemish, inspected for days, killed by the whole assembly, its blood marking the
doorframe, *not one bone broken*. Fourteen centuries later John stands at a cross and watches
soldiers break two men's legs and stop at the third — "that the scripture should be fulfilled,
A bone of him shall not be broken." The lamb was always a portrait.

## Movement 1 — The Picture

It is the last night of Israel's slavery in Egypt. Four hundred years of bondage, and judgment is
finally falling — not on Israel, but on the nation that enslaved them. And on this night of all
nights, God does not tell His people to fight, or to flee, or to hide. He tells them to kill a lamb.

Every household, one lamb. Not a symbol chosen at random — a specific animal, with specific rules,
written down before a single firstborn had died. Read them now, and they will sound oddly precise.
Almost as if they were describing something — or someone — else.

## Movement 2 — The Problem

Because death is coming to every house in Egypt. "I will pass through the land of Egypt this night,"
God says, "and will smite all the firstborn." Every home. And here is the part we rush past: God does
not say the Israelites are automatically safe. Their address does not save them. Their bloodline does
not save them. Death is coming to *their* doors too.

Something has to stand between the family and the judgment. Someone has to die in that house tonight —
either the firstborn, or a substitute. That is the problem the lamb exists to answer.

## Movement 3 — The Strange Detail

Now listen to how carefully the lamb is described.

**"Your lamb shall be without blemish, a male of the first year."** Not the leftover of the flock.
The flawless one.

It is not killed the moment it is chosen. **"In the tenth day of this month they shall take to them
every man a lamb"** — and **"ye shall keep it up until the fourteenth day of the same month."** Taken
into the home on the tenth, lived with, watched, until the fourteenth — four days. Time enough to be
certain there was nothing wrong with it. Inspected, and found faultless.

Then, **"the whole assembly of the congregation of Israel shall kill it in the evening."** Not one
executioner — the whole people share the act.

And the blood does not go on an altar. It goes on the house. **"They shall take of the blood, and
strike it on the two side posts and on the upper door post…"** Two uprights, and a beam across the
top. A doorway, marked in blood, that a family walks through to be safe.

And one rule that makes no practical sense for a meal: **"neither shall ye break a bone thereof."**
You may roast it, eat it, finish it before morning — but you must not break a single bone.

Hold that detail. A spotless male lamb, inspected and found faultless, killed by the whole assembly,
its blood on a frame of wood, not one bone broken. Then God says the words the whole night turns on:
**"When I see the blood, I will pass over you, and the plague shall not be upon you to destroy you."**

Not "when I see how good you are." Not "when I see your effort." *When I see the blood.*

## Movement 4 — The Centuries-Early Match

Now move forward roughly fourteen hundred years, to a hill outside Jerusalem — and notice *when* it
happens. Jesus is crucified at Passover, the very feast where every family in the city is killing a lamb.

He dies. And John, standing there, records something strange. To hasten death the soldiers came to
break the men's legs. They broke the legs of the two crucified beside Him. **"But when they came to
Jesus, and saw that he was dead already, they brake not his legs."** And John tells you exactly why
it mattered: **"For these things were done, that the scripture should be fulfilled, A bone of him
shall not be broken."**

The same rule. *Neither shall ye break a bone thereof.* A rule about a lamb in Egypt, fulfilled at
a cross — by Roman soldiers with no thought of Exodus, only doing their job.

And the New Testament does not leave the connection for us to guess. Paul says it plainly:
**"Christ our passover is sacrificed for us."** Peter calls Him **"a lamb without blemish and without
spot."** Without blemish. Inspected, like the Passover lamb — even Pilate examined Him and could find
no fault in Him. Faultless, and killed anyway, so that judgment would pass over a guilty house.

## Movement 5 — The Honest Objection

Now, be fair to the doubt. Someone will say: every ancient culture sacrificed animals. Of course a
Christian can find a lamb in the Old Testament and call it Jesus. Isn't this just reading the ending
back into the beginning?

It is a fair question, so weigh the actual details. It is not merely "a sacrifice." It is a *spotless*
one, *inspected* and found faultless. It is blood applied to a *doorframe of wood* — and the family is
spared not by their worth but by the blood. It is the one bone-rule that has no reason in a meal, landing
on a cross centuries later, named by an eyewitness as fulfilled prophecy. It is the timing — the Lamb
of God dying at the exact feast of the lamb. Any one of those could be coincidence. All of them, woven
through one ritual God commanded before the first Passover ever happened, is not a coincidence. It is a
design finally stepping into the light.

## Movement 6 — The Exchange

And here is the heart of it. In Egypt, the lamb did not make the family *better*. It died *instead* of
the firstborn. A life for a life. The blood said: death has already visited this house — it fell on the
lamb, so it will pass over the child.

That is what the cross is. Not God overlooking sin, but God providing the substitute Himself. "Christ
our passover is sacrificed *for us.*" In your place. The faultless One, so that the judgment you and I
have earned would look at the blood — and pass over.

You were not redeemed, Peter says, with silver or gold, "but with the precious blood of Christ." It
cost the Lamb everything. It is offered to you for nothing.

## Movement 7 — The Invitation

There is one more thing about that first Passover. The blood on the doorpost did no good *outside* the
house. It had to be applied. A family could believe the lamb was spotless, agree every rule was kept —
and still lose the firstborn, if the blood was never put on their own door.

The blood of Christ is not on a doorframe now. It is held out to you. Not earned by your record, not
deserved by your effort — received, the way a frightened family in Egypt simply trusted the blood and
stayed under it through the dark.

When God looks at your life, what will He see? Your striving — or the blood of the Lamb? The whole of
Exodus 12 was God teaching the world to hope for one sentence: *when I see the blood, I will pass over.*
He still means it. Come under the blood of the Lamb who was slain for you — and let the judgment pass over.
