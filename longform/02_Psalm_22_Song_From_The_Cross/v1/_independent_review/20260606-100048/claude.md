## Real problems

**1. "screamed seven words into the dark" (Movement 1) — factual miscount.**
The line he screams is *"My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"* — count them: My/God/my/God/why/hast/thou/forsaken/me = **nine** words in English. The Aramaic (*Eli, Eli, lama sabachthani*) is **four**. "Seven" matches nothing. A first-hearing listener counts nine right after you say seven. Either drop the number ("screamed into the dark") or fix it.

**2. "says one word: It is finished" (Movement 7) — same miscount, opposite direction.**
"It is finished" is **three** English words. It's only "one word" in the Greek (*tetelestai*) — which the spoken script never tells the listener. So the naive listener hears "one word" then hears three. This is the twin of the seven-words flaw, and the brief's whole audience is "zero Bible knowledge." Either say "one word in the original" or rephrase.

**3. "He was naming the whole song" (Movement 5) — load-bearing overclaim stated as flat fact.**
*"When a first-century Jew quoted the opening line of a psalm, he was not quoting a line. He was naming the whole song."* This is the hinge of the entire fulfilment argument, and it's presented as settled. It is **not** uncontested — that quoting an incipit deliberately invokes the *entire content* (including the resurrection turn) is a popular apologetic inference, not an established first-century fact. The ledger calls it "well-attested"; that's stronger than the scholarship supports. Movement 4 models the right move ("an honest objection… not a sales pitch") — give this claim the same honesty. Soften to "he was reaching for the whole song" framed as the most natural reading, or briefly ground it, rather than asserting it as fact.

**4. Internal tension: "the forsaking was real" vs the verse you quote in Movement 6.**
Movement 7 asserts twice that *"the forsaking was real."* But Movement 6 quotes Psalm 22:24: *"…neither hath he hid his face from him; but when he cried unto him, he heard."* The psalm Jesus is invoking explicitly says God did **not** hide his face and **did** hear. You can reconcile this (the judicial cry of vv1–21 resolved by the turn at v22), and that's exactly the psalm's structure — but the script never does the reconciling. A skeptic or a careful believer will press: "your own quoted verse contradicts your 'forsaking was real' claim." One sentence acknowledging that the same psalm answers the cry would close the gap and actually strengthen the penal-substitution beat.
