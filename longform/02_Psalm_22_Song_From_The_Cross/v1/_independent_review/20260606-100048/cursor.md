Independent adversarial review of the Psalm 22 long-form narration.

## Doctrinal soundness

**Psalm 22:31 → “It is finished” is over-pressed.** Movement 7 calls the clipped quote *“three words about a finished work”* and says *“the same finished work gets the same verdict.”* In full KJV, Psalm 22:31 reads: *“They shall come, and shall declare his righteousness unto a people that shall be born, that he hath done this.”* The proximate antecedent of *“his righteousness”* and *“he hath done this”* is God’s accomplished deliverance, not the sufferer’s self-declared completion. John 19:30’s *“It is finished”* is first-person (*tetelestai*); the psalm line is third-person. Thematic resonance is defensible in evangelical preaching; presenting it as *“the same verdict”* is an exegetical slide the companion Isaiah film was careful not to make.

**Resurrection at v22 is asserted harder than the text warrants on its own.** Movement 6: *“A dead man does not declare anything. The sufferer is alive again, on his feet, praising.”* That is argument, not quotation. Psalm 22’s pivot can be read as anticipated rescue within the lament form (David himself is the “I” who would go on praising). The ledger cites Hebrews 2:12 — the strongest NT warrant — but **it is never spoken**. Compared to the locked Isaiah 53 movement, which says Isaiah leaves *“a shape that only resurrection fills”* and explicitly refuses to prove resurrection from bare OT text, this script **states resurrection as settled fact from v22 alone**. Orthodox landing, but the spoken case is thinner than the metadata claims.

**Incipit convention is stated as universal fact.** Movement 5: *“When a first-century Jew quoted the opening line of a psalm, he was not quoting a line. He was naming the whole song.”* Standard evangelical reading; not unanimous scholarship (some take the cry as anguished reuse of familiar words). For a documentary claiming *“honest objection”* posture elsewhere, this line has zero hedge.

**“Nothing in David’s life ever matched it”** (Movement 2) is too absolute. David was hunted, mocked, betrayed; Shimei’s curses parallel elements of vv6–8. The script needs David’s death to be unlike crucifixion — fair — but *“nothing… ever matched”* hands skeptics an easy rebuttal and undercuts Movement 4’s *“honest objection”* frame.

**Unverifiable embellishment:** *“The soldiers throwing dice had never read Psalm twenty-two.”* You cannot know that. It weakens the forensic tone built in Movement 3.

## First-hearing clarity

**“Seven words” contradicts what the ear hears.** Cold open: *“screamed seven words into the dark”* → immediately followed by a nine-word English line: *“My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?”* A listener with no Bible background counts words and loses trust in the first 10 seconds. Worse: *“seven words”* may invoke the “Seven Last Sayings” tradition, which this line is not.

**“So that you would never have to”** (Movement 7) never completes the thought. Never have to what? Be forsaken? Suffer? Die? A zero-knowledge listener cannot supply the soteriology. The Isaiah 53 independent panel flagged the same unfinished-benefit structure (*“so that you would not have to be”*) as ambiguous and potentially promising suffering-exemption; this companion repeats the pattern.

**“John records two words”** — John is never introduced (apostle, Gospel writer, eyewitness). Matthew and John appear only as citation tags in stage directions, not in spoken narration. First-time listeners may not know who “John” is.

**Clipped Psalm 22:31** — *“…he hath done this”* — leaves *“he”* unidentified at the moment of maximum rhetorical weight. The listener has not heard *“declare his righteousness.”*

**Movement 4 → Movement 5 whiplash on “pierced.”** Movement 4 fairly concedes the Masoretic *“like a lion”* reading and says *“Take pierced off the table entirely, just for a moment.”* Movement 5 immediately: *“He was claiming the pierced hands as his own hands.”* Movement 7: *“The hands that were pierced were spread out wide enough to reach the ends of the world.”* Logically consistent (case doesn’t rest on one word; Jesus still claims the reading), but **on first hearing it sounds like you conceded the point and then smuggled it back in.**

---

## Grace-anchored conviction

**The grace lines land well:** *“You do not have to make yourself worth saving before you come”* and *“not with what you must do, but with what he hath done”* are strong, Isaiah-aligned, non-works invitations.

**But the penal-substitution line violates the same rule the Isaiah 53 lock fixed.** Movement 7: *“the forsaking was real — so that you would never have to.”* That is benefit-avoidance / transaction framing — what the locked Isaiah companion replaced with explicit bearing language: *“wounded for your transgressions, and bore them in your place.”* The ledger even doubles down: *“the sinless Son bears the God-forsakenness our sin earned.”* Doctrinally evangelical; rhetorically it is the exact gain-loss cadence your own panel rejected in the sister film.

**No fear-selling** — good. No works ladder — good.

---

## Freshness = faithful depth

**Genuine strengths:** Movement 4’s pierced steel-man is unusually honest for this genre. The incipit thesis is the right fresh entry. The v27 worldwide hinge is text-faithful depth.

**Freshness risks:** The Ps 22:31 / *tetelestai* parallel and the hard resurrection read at v22 feel like apologetic compression — surprising in presentation, not always earned word-by-word from the text. The Isaiah pilot was more disciplined here.

---

## One thread spine

**Mostly holds:** cry → David’s unlived death → forensic wounds → honest pierced objection → Jesus claims whole psalm → turn → finished-work landing.

**Minor thread stress:** Movement 4’s poetry concession (*“David… reached for vivid images — ‘dogs,’ a ‘lion’”*) coexists with Movement 3’s *“witness statement”* certainty. The script wants both forensic and poetic frames without reconciling them for the listener — only for the skeptical reader in Movement 4.

---

## Landing

**Penultimate lines do new work** — the Psalm 22:31 grace inversion (*“what he hath done”*) and the v27 *“ends of the world is you”* direct address are specific to this text.

**Final line is weaker:** *“Will you turn, and join the congregation that praises him?”* echoes v27/v22 legitimately, but after a 6–7 minute documentary it lands close to a standard invitation. Not as tired as *“will you trust Him?”* — but the closing question does less new work than the line before it.

---

## Hook (~5 seconds)

**Viscerally strong** — cross, ninth hour, inability to breathe. Grips immediately.

**Undermined by the seven/nine-word mismatch** in the same breath as the hook promise.

VERDICT: REVISE
TOP FIXES:
1. Replace *“the forsaking was real — so that you would never have to”* with explicit, completed substitution language (mirror the locked Isaiah 53 pattern: bore forsakenness / transgression *in your place*), and clarify what was borne so a first-time listener is not left hanging.
2. Rebuild Movement 7’s Psalm 22:31 → *“It is finished”* link: either quote enough of v31 that *“he”* is grounded, or frame it as thematic resonance (not *“the same verdict”*); align resurrection tone in Movement 6 with Isaiah 53’s conservative hint-or-add spoken Hebrews 2:12.
3. Fix first-hearing glitches in the cold open (*“seven words”* vs nine English words) and pierced rhetorical order: either qualify *“pierced”* when it returns after Movement 4, or restructure so the steel-man comes before the emotional peak on v16.
