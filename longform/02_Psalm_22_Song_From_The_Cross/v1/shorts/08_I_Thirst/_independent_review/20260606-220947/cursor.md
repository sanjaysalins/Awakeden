### Genuine residual issues

#### 1. **Ps 69 landmine — implicit fulfilment via series-shape + hook (MODERATE, load-bearing)**

The spoken text avoids the explicit error. But the **structure trains the wrong inference**:

- Short #1 (locked): psalm detail → John at the cross → `"that the scripture might be fulfilled"`.
- This short: psalm thirst detail → John at the cross → `"I thirst."`

A listener shaped by #1 — or any listener who knows John 19:28 is a fulfilment moment — can easily **supply Psalm 22 as John's fulfilment referent**, even though John 19:28's fulfilment clause standardly points to **Psalm 69:21** (vinegar), not 22:15.

The hook makes that worse:

> `"A thousand years before the cross, a song wrote down the killing thirst of a dying man. At the cross, Jesus lived it — in two words."`

**`"Jesus lived it"`** is fulfilment-coded. Locked sister shorts do not use this move:

- #2: `"Matthew records both"` → `"The same gesture. The same taunt"` (parallel, not "lived").
- #7: `"the dying man of his song bears the marks of one — written early"` (picture/match, not "lived").

This short never delivers an equivalent hedge after both texts. For the **trickiest 🟡 beat in the series**, that is a real gap — not a nit.

#### 2. **"In two words" tease misstates what Ps 22 actually predicts (MODERATE, clarity)**

Psalm 22:15 **pictures** killing thirst (`"strength dried up… tongue cleaveth… dust of death"`). It does **not** contain the phrase `"I thirst."`

Unlike #2's verbal match (`"let him deliver him"` in both psalm and Matthew), this beat is **thematic only**.

Yet the hook's **`"in two words"`** front-loads a **verbal-prophecy** tease before the listener hears the long Ps 22:15 quote. On first hearing, that can sound like the psalm predicted the exact cross-words — which overclaims the text. The paraphrase (`"Dried like a shard… tongue stuck fast"`) helps, but it comes **after** the misleading tease.

#### 3. **Missing David-distance bridge (MINOR, regression from #7)**

#7 (locked) explicitly says:

> `"David wrote it in the first person, but he was describing someone else's death, not his own."`

#8 only has:

> `"David pictures the dying man's body giving out"`

For a zero-Bible listener, **who the dying man is** (David? Jesus? a generic sufferer?) is less clear here than in the prior short. Small, but real clarity friction.

#### 4. **"John" is unexplained (MINOR)**

> `"John records what the dying Jesus said"`

No series context is spoken. A cold listener may not know John is the Gospel writer/eyewitness. Prior long-form review flagged the same pattern. Not doctrinal, but clarity.

#### 5. **Living-water block — scoped correctly, slightly stacked (WATCH, not FAIL)**

> `"Think of who is saying it. The One who made every river and spring — the One who offers the whole world living water — hung there with nothing, thirsting, in your place."`

**`"Think of who is saying it"`** properly frames this as devotional reflection, not Ps 22 content. No living-water over-read into the psalm.

Minor friction: Creator (John 1:3) + living water (John 4/7) + substitution land in one breath — dense for a cold listener, but not unfaithful.

#### 6. **Quoted `"thou hast brought me into the dust of death"` — unused thread (WATCH)**

The verse attributes the descent to God (`"thou hast brought me"`). The script paraphrases physical symptoms, then pivots to water-irony/substitution without touching divine agency. Not wrong for 60s; missed faithful depth, not error.
