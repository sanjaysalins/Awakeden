## Independent adversarial review — *He Hath Done This* (Psalm 22 short #5)

### What holds up (only to narrow the hunt)

- **KJV words inside quotes:** `"…that he hath done this."` matches the final clause of Ps 22:31 in your cache. `"It is finished"` matches John 19:30; period correctly sits **outside** the quote. No altered KJV lexemes inside quotation marks.
- **Resonance guard is spoken:** `"Different words, in different tongues, a thousand years apart — but the same note: a finished work."` — this does the load-bearing work; no Hebrew/Greek pun, no word-count gimmick, no “paid in full” cadence.
- **Grace landing:** `"Nothing left for you to earn, nothing left to prove, nothing to add"` is invitation-shaped, not fear/transaction framing.
- **Thread:** hook → Ps 22:31 → John 19:30 → resonance disclaimer → “what was finished?” → CTA. No thread swap.

---

### Findings (specific, cited)

**1. Misattribution — “His final words:” (accuracy + first-hearing)**

> *"David ends the song looking ahead — to God's saving work, accomplished, and told to a people not yet born. **His final words:**"*

Then you quote `"…that he hath done this."`

Ps 22:31 in full is: *“They shall come, and shall declare his righteousness unto a people that shall be born, that he hath done this.”* That is **not** David speaking a last line in his own mouth. It is third-person prophecy about **future generations** declaring **God’s** righteousness. The setup actually says that (“told to a people not yet born”), then contradicts itself with “His final words.” A zero-Bible listener will think David personally said “he hath done this” as his closing breath — which the text does not do. Your own hook gets this right (“**The last line of Psalm twenty-two**”); line 23 does not.

**2. “The last word Jesus spoke on the cross” — over-broad before John is named (accuracy #1)**

> Hook: *"**the last word Jesus spoke on the cross**"*
> Later: *"He breathed **one last word**:"* → `"It is finished".`

John 19:30 supports this **in John’s narrative**. Luke 23:46 records another loud cry — *“Father, into thy hands I commend my spirit”* — also at death. Calling it the cross’s “last word” **before** you anchor the claim to John invites a harmonization pushback the project says it cares about. The brief scopes the thesis to John 19:30; the **spoken hook does not**. That is a residual accuracy slip, not a nit.

**3. Hook overclaims relative to your own guard — “very same truth” vs “same note”**

> Hook: *"land on the **very same truth**"*
> Guard line: *"**the same note**: a finished work."*

The long-form’s external panel already forced a downgrade from “same verdict” to “same note” because Ps 22:31’s *“he hath done this”* is **third-person** about God’s accomplished deliverance, while John 19:30 is **first-person** completion on the cross. “Very same truth” at the open is stronger than the guarded middle — it sounds like exegetical identity, not thematic resonance. That is the exact slide your 🟡 guard exists to prevent.

**4. Landing collapses the Ps 22:31 pronoun guard**

> *"He has done it — all of it. **That's Jesus.** Come to Him."*

After a clipped quote where **“he”** in context is God’s saving action (your ledger even says: *“God’s righteous saving work”*), the landing reassigns “He has done it” straight to Jesus without any bridging line. Doctrinally defensible in christological preaching; **as a resonance-only short that just told us the words differ**, this reads like the Psalm line **was** about Jesus all along — i.e. a quiet verbal-fulfilment re-entry at the close. The long-form fixed this by saying Jesus *“reaches the same note,”* not *“That’s Jesus”* on the Psalm pronoun.

**5. Minor first-hearing fuzz — standalone “Done.”**

> After Ps quote: *"**Done.** And when Jesus had carried the cross…"*

Immediately after `"…that he hath done this."`, a lone *“Done.”* can sound like you’re equating the English words before you’ve delivered the guard line. Not fatal — the next beat fixes it — but on one pass it briefly undercuts the “different words” discipline.

**6. CTA is thin compared to the beat’s promise**

> *"That's Jesus. **Come to Him.**"*

Not a forbidden “will you trust Him?” clone, but after a strong “what was finished?” answer, *“Come to Him”* does less **new** work than the long-form’s congregation/welcome close tied to Ps 22:27/31. Acceptable for 60s; still the weakest beat relative to “landing does new work.”

**7. Non-blocking pedantry (flag only if you’re tightening)**

- *"when Jesus had **carried the cross all the way to the end**"* — Synoptics have Simon of Cyrene carrying the beam; this is metaphorical at best, literal at worst.
- Hook assumes series context: *“Psalm twenty-two”* is opaque to a cold listener (less fatal for short #5 in a series).
