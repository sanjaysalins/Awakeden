# Independent review — grok (OK, 68s)

**Findings (adversarial review against the stated lens):**

**KJV verbatim failures (spoken script):**

- The Psalm 22:7-8 block is delivered as:  
  "…they shake the head saying, He trusted on the LORD that he would deliver him: let him deliver him…"  
  Actual KJV 22:7 ends: "they shake the head, saying," (comma after "head"). The spoken version removes the comma and glues "head saying." The sourcing note concedes the comma is "presentation" but the delivered line still alters the text the listener hears. The lens requires every word inside quotation marks to be exact KJV.

- The landing split:  
  "He saved others," they jeered, "himself he cannot save."  
  KJV Matt 27:42 is one clause with a semicolon: "He saved others; himself he cannot save." The script changes the semicolon to a comma inside the first set of quotes and inserts narrator speech between the two fragments. Words inside the quotation marks are no longer presented exactly as they stand in the KJV; the punctuation is adapted for dramatic delivery.

- Matt 27:43 quote is clipped with ellipsis ("He trusted in God; let him deliver him now…"), which the sourcing discloses. However, the first two quoted blocks already violate the "exact KJV inside quotation marks" rule.

**Clarity on first hearing (zero-Bible-knowledge listener):**

- "They were right: He wouldn't come down — because He was saving you."  
  This is the core reframe of Matt 27:42. On first hearing it concedes the mockers' taunt ("they were right") before redefining "cannot" as "would not." A listener hears the narration momentarily agree with the jeer that Jesus could not save himself, then pivot. This is a logic trick that creates momentary self-contradiction for the ear. The preceding "Almost word for word. The mockers were reciting a script they didn't know existed" sets up irony, but the "They were right" line undercuts clarity rather than sharpening it.

- "They dared God to rescue Him — and God didn't. Not because He lacked the power, but because rescuing Himself would have meant losing you."  
  The bald "God didn't" lands before the qualification. For a first-time listener the sequence registers as "the mockers dared God and God actually failed to act / abandoned Him," followed by a quick theological patch. The hook already introduced the cross as the place where the taunt happened; this line risks reinforcing the very scandal the short is trying to resolve.

**Grace-anchored conviction and landing:**

- The substitution language ("because He was saving you") is positive and avoids crude fear/gain-loss. However, "rescuing Himself would have meant losing you" personalizes the listener into the divine dilemma in a way that can read as slightly manipulative emotional appeal, even if not full works or self-interest framing. The final CTA ("That's Jesus. Come to Him.") is terse and does almost no new work. The reframe carries the weight; the close itself is the standard short-form imperative with a name drop. The lens requires the landing to do new work, not just tack a generic invitation onto a good preceding sentence.

**Thread spine and hook:**

- Hook is specific and grips inside 5 seconds ("A thousand years before the cross, David wrote down the words Jesus' enemies would hurl at Him — and the way they'd shake their heads doing it"). Strong.
- Spine holds (prophetic taunt/gesture match → ironic recitation at the cross → reframe of the "cannot" line). No thread-swapping.
- However, the "God didn't" sentence introduces a theodicy question that the landing then answers. This is a small detour rather than pure forward motion on the single spine.

**Doctrinal soundness / no overclaim:**

- The spoken narration honors the critical guard: it never says Matthew cited the psalm as fulfilled here. It only claims verbal/gestural match ("Almost word for word… a script they didn't know existed"). The metadata is explicit about this limit.
- "Predictive correspondence" is asserted in the non-spoken section as "undisputed." The spoken text stays within that. No invented doctrine or contrarian reading is smuggled into the narration itself. The voluntary-substitution landing ("He WOULD not… had the power") is orthodox and within evangelical bounds.
- The only doctrinal-adjacent risk is the clarity problems above: a listener could temporarily hear the short as saying the mockers correctly diagnosed Jesus' inability, or that God simply let the dare succeed.

**Other notes (lower weight):**

- "Psalm twenty-two. David, describing a mocked and dying man…" assumes the listener will accept that this figure is the one whose enemies' words Jesus would hear. The hook has already named "Jesus' enemies," so it mostly connects, but a zero-knowledge listener gets very little bridging before the Matthew quote arrives.
- The multi-voice note in the artifact is sensible; keeping everything narrator for the accuracy pass is defensible.

The problems are concentrated in two areas the lens explicitly prioritizes: (1) strict KJV verbatim inside quotation marks (punctuation and quote-splitting), and (2) first-hearing clarity around the ironic twist and the "God didn't" phrasing. These are fixable but real.

VERDICT: REVISE
TOP FIXES:
1. Restore exact KJV wording and punctuation in all quoted blocks; eliminate mid-quote narrator insertions and adapted commas (fix "they shake the head, saying," and quote the Matt 27:42 line as a continuous unit or attribute cleanly outside the quotes).
2. Remove or rephrase "They were right: He wouldn't come down" — the blunt concession to the mockers creates a logic twist that confuses first-time listeners; make the reframe direct without momentarily validating the taunt.
3. Rephrase or immediately guard "They dared God to rescue Him — and God didn't" so the stark claim does not land as divine inaction or abandonment before the "because He was saving you" purpose is clear.
