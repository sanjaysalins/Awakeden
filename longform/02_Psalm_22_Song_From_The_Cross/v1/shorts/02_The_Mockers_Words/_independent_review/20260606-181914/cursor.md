### Doctrinal soundness

**Voluntary substitution landing is orthodox and well-aimed.** Reframing mockers' **"himself he cannot save"** as **would not**, not **could not**, is standard evangelical reading; Matt 26:53 support is implied, not invented.

**Problems:**

1. **Matthew scene conflation (accuracy under ACCURACY #1).**  
   *"Matthew describes the crowd 'wagging their heads' and sneering"* fuses two Matthew scenes:
   - **Matt 27:39** — passers-by reviling, **wagging their heads**
   - **Matt 27:41–43** — chief priests, scribes, elders — **"let him deliver him now"** / **"himself he cannot save"**

   Psalm 22:7–8 unifies gesture + taunt in one voice. Matthew spreads them across different mockers. Saying **"the crowd"** did both on first hearing implies one group, one moment — **Matthew does not say that.** The correspondence still holds textually; the **spoken fusion overstates what Matthew narrates.**

2. **Fulfilment-citation guard — letter honored, spirit strained.**  
   Metadata correctly notes Matthew never writes *"that the scripture might be fulfilled."* But spoken lines still push fulfilment:
   - *"David wrote down the words Jesus' enemies **would hurl**"*
   - *"reciting a **script** they didn't know existed"*

   Metadata goes further: *"unknowingly recited **the prophecy** that marks Him as the One."*  
   That is predictive-fulfilment rhetoric without Matthew's citation formula. For a brief built on **gestural/verbal match only**, *"script"* / *"prophecy"* / *"would hurl"* are overclaims relative to the stated guard.

3. **"Almost word for word" overstates the back-to-back quotes.**  
   Immediately after hearing:
   - *"He trusted **on the LORD** that he would deliver him: let him deliver him"*
   - *"He trusted **in God**; let him deliver him **now**"*

   …the narrator says **"Almost word for word."** The kernel **"let him deliver him"** matches; the openings do not. A listener who just heard both lines may feel the script oversold the verbal match. Safer claim: gesture match + repeated taunt-phrase — not "almost word for word" on the full lines quoted.

### Clarity on first hearing

**Hook is strong for Bible-literate viewers** (~5s): *"A thousand years before the cross, David wrote down the words Jesus' enemies would hurl at Him."* Concrete, provocative, on-brief.

**Fails partially on "zero Bible knowledge":**
- **David**, **Psalm twenty-two**, **Matthew**, **the cross** — four assumed reference points before any onboarding.
- **"They were right"** is a logic trick the lens flags. Mockers claim impotence (**"cannot save"**); narrator affirms (**"They were right: He wouldn't come down"**). First hearing: *Were the mockers right about Jesus?* The cannot/wouldn't pivot requires a second pass. Clever; confusing.
- **"God didn't"** (*"They dared God to rescue Him — and God didn't"*) lands as divine failure for one beat before the power/substitution repair. Repair works, but the dare/fail line is a stumble.

### Grace-anchored conviction

**Good:** Landing is invitation (*"Come to Him"*), not coercion; substitution is framed as **saving you**, not threatening loss of salvation.

**Borderline charter violation:** *"rescuing Himself would have meant **losing you**"* is transactional gain/loss framing — a rescue-or-forfeit trade. Constitution (binding): **"No gain/loss framing… what you lose if you ignore this."** This line frames the atonement as **Him vs. you** economics. Doctrinally common; **charter-tension** under a strict grace-anchored read. Prefer: He stayed because **that was the rescue** — without the "losing you" ledger.

**CTA:** *"That's Jesus. Come to Him."* — landing reframe does new work; the final two words are generic short-template close, not fresh landing work on their own.

TOP FIXES:
