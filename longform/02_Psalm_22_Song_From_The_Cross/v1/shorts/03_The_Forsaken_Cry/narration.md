**[narrator]**
The loneliest sentence Jesus ever spoke wasn't His own line — He was quoting.

**[narrator]**
Psalm twenty-two opens in the voice of a forsaken man. David wrote it; Jesus made it His own.

**[narrator]**
David's very first line:

**[narrator — KJV, Psalm 22:1]**
"My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"

**[narrator]**
A thousand years later, at the ninth hour, Jesus cried it from the cross:

**[narrator — KJV, Matthew 27:46]**
"My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"

**[narrator]**
The very same words.

**[narrator]**
This was no cry of lost faith — even forsaken, He still calls God "my God." It was the sinless Son taking our place, and bearing the forsaking that our sin deserved.

**[narrator]**
He was forsaken so that you never will be. However far you've run, the way home is open — and He opened it from the dark.

## DEPTH & SOURCING  *(not spoken)*
- **KJV verbatim** (self-verified via `pipeline/scripture.fetch_kjv`, 2026-06-06):
  - Ps 22:1: "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" — exact; a complete interrogative clause ending at the
    KJV question mark (the verse continues "why art thou so far…", so no mid-clause clip — no trailing ellipsis needed).
  - Matt 27:46: "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" — exact KJV English, a complete sentence ending
    the verse (the "that is to say" rendering Matthew gives). NO ellipsis (panel-flagged: an ellipsis inside the
    quote was an editorial mark inside KJV text) — the narrator frame "Jesus cried it from the cross" supplies
    the context; the Aramaic preamble is simply not quoted. Identical string to Ps 22:1 — which is the point.
  - "my God" (conviction) — KJV phrase from the same verse.
- **The link is the STRONGEST in the series:** the mockers (short #2) unknowingly MATCHED the psalm; here Jesus
  deliberately QUOTES it. No textual variant on either side.
- **Doctrinal guards (from the long-form):**
  - **NOT "despair":** the short explicitly denies the sinful-hopelessness reading — "no cry of lost faith — even
    forsaken, He still calls God 'my God.'" (Christ's faith holds in the dereliction; He claims God as His God.)
  - **Penal substitution via the forsaking, in BEARING language** (not the gain/loss "so you would never have to"
    cadence the Isaiah panel removed): "the sinless Son taking our place, and with it the forsaking our sin
    deserved." The judicial God-forsakenness borne as our substitute — NOT a claim that the Trinity was severed.
  - **Grace landing:** "forsaken — so that you never will be" is the substitution PROMISE (cf. Heb 13:5 "I will
    never leave thee, nor forsake thee" — believers are never forsaken because Christ was). Positive: "bring you home."
  - **Does NOT overclaim the incipit/"whole psalm" inference** (the long-form softened that; a 60s cut leans only
    on the verbatim cry itself).
