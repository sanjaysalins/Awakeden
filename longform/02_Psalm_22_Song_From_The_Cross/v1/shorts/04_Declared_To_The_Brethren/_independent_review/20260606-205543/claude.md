I've reviewed the narration against the lens. Here are my specific findings.

## KJV verbatim — both quoted spans check out
- **Ps 22:22** — "I will declare thy name unto my brethren: in the midst of the congregation will I praise thee." Exact KJV, full verse. ✓
- **Heb 2:11** — "not ashamed to call them brethren" is the exact KJV phrase (verse: "…he is not ashamed to call them brethren,"). ✓
- The DEPTH note's claim that the *opening* phrase "I will declare thy name unto my brethren" is identical in Ps 22:22 and Heb 2:12 is correct, and the divergent tail (LXX "church / sing praise" vs psalm "congregation / praise thee") is real — so **referencing** Heb 2:12 instead of re-quoting it is the right call. No conflation. ✓

## Resurrection claim — NT-warranted, not over-read
The bridge is sound: the claim rests on Hebrews 2:12 *attributing* Ps 22:22 to Christ, plus the psalm's own death→living-praise pivot (the psalm does carry death imagery — v15 "the dust of death"). "A dead man declares nothing. So the New Testament takes that very line and puts it in the mouth of the risen Jesus" anchors the resurrection in the NT, not in the bare psalm. No over-read. ✓

## Residual flags (genuine, minor)

**1. "risen Jesus" is an interpretive compression of Hebrews' "exalted/glorified."** Hebrews 2:9 says "crowned with glory and honour" after "the suffering of death" — it presupposes resurrection rather than stating "risen." Calling the speaker of 2:12 "the risen Jesus" is defensible shorthand (exaltation entails resurrection), but it is one inferential step. Acceptable for a 60s short; noting it so the panel isn't surprised.

**2. Closing order risks a faint universalist/presumptive read.** "now stands alive, unashamed to call **you** His own. … Come to Him." In Hebrews the "brethren" are "they who are sanctified" (2:11) — belonging is conferred on those who come (cf. John 1:12), not on every hearer. The narration asserts the belonging to the listener as present fact, *then* invites. The "Come to Him" keeps it an offer (per the guard), so this is not an error — but the sequence (belong → then come) is the one spot a strict ear could hear universalism. Tightening would make it airtight, e.g. let the invitation condition the belonging: *"Come to Him — and the risen One is unashamed to call you His own."*

**3. "from death to a man standing alive, praising God in a crowd"** edges toward implying the *psalm itself* narrates resurrection. The very next lines correct it by routing the resurrection through Hebrews, so it lands fine — but the phrase in isolation leans on the structural pivot harder than the bare text proves. Already mitigated; no change required.

Everything else holds: single thread (cross → resurrection → family) is consistent hook-to-CTA, the hook grips ("Most people stop reading it there — and miss where it actually ends"), grace-anchored (no fear/gain-loss), and the landing does new work ("not in a grave, but in a family"). Item 2 is the only one I'd actually act on, and it's a tightening, not a defect.
