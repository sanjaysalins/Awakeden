## Doctrinal soundness — sound, with one load-bearing pressure point
- Removing the 22:16 "pierced" line and resting 100% on the garments/22:18 → John 19:24 is the right call. The proof is now genuinely airtight: John *explicitly* names this verse as fulfilled. No contested/contrarian reading survives. ✅
- **Pressure point (the one real flag):** the whole apologetic weight sits on *"both… to the letter"* / "TWO acts." Hebrew poetic parallelism is often *synonymous* — "part my garments" and "cast lots upon my vesture" can be read as one act described twice. The "two distinct acts" reading is supplied by John 19:23–24 (four-way division of the outer garments **then** lots for the seamless coat). It IS defensible — you're echoing John's own distinction, not inventing one — but the script states it as if the *Psalm* self-evidently predicts two acts. It doesn't on its own; John does. The DEPTH ledger handles this honestly, but the spoken line claims more precision from the Hebrew than the Hebrew alone gives. This is the only place a hostile scholar gets traction. Defensible, not airtight.

## Clarity on first hearing — good, two watch-items
- The hook paradox *"a death he would never die"* could momentarily confuse a zero-Bible listener, but it self-resolves within ~4 seconds (*"it never happened to him"*). Acceptable as a grab device.
- *"gamble for them"* / *"threw dice for the rest"* — casting lots ≠ dice/gambling in the strict sense (lots were closer to drawing straws / discerning a verdict). For a lay audience it reads fine and aids clarity, but it's a mild anachronistic characterization layered on top of a prophecy-precision claim — slightly self-undercutting since the whole point is *exactness*. Consider "cast lots for the rest" to keep the precision claim clean.
