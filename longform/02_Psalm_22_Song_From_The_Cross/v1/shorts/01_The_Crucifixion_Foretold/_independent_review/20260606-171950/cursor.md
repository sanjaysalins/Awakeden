## Independent adversarial review — v4 narration

### What v4 fixed (credit where due, then move on)

Removing the pierced quote and resting proof on Psalm 22:18 + John 19:24’s explicit fulfillment citation is the right call given “accuracy #1.” David-pronoun clarity (“it never happened to him; he died an old man”) is a real improvement. No fear/gain-loss framing. One thread holds: garments prophecy → cross fulfillment → planned death → intentional Jesus → invitation.

The remaining problems are smaller but real — especially under an accuracy-first brief.

---

### KJV verbatim

**Psalm 22:18 — PASS.** Exact against project cache:

> "They part my garments among them, and cast lots upon my vesture."

**John 19:24 phrase — WORDS pass, PUNCTUATION fails strict KJV.**

Spoken line:
> `"that the scripture might be fulfilled."`

KJV John 19:24 continues with a **comma**, not a period:
> `that the scripture might be fulfilled, which saith, They parted my raiment among them...`

The words inside the marks are exact; the terminal period presents a clipped mid-verse clause as a finished KJV sentence. On a project where “every word inside quotation marks must be exact KJV,” this is a flag — not altered *words*, but altered *presentation* of the verse’s form.

No other quoted spans. Paraphrases (“gamble,” “split Jesus’ clothes,” “threw dice”) are outside quotes — fine.

### Doctrinal soundness

No invented doctrine, no contrarian pierced-variant chase, no clickbait reading. Messianic reading of Psalm 22 is standard evangelical. “It was the plan” / “walked to it on purpose” is orthodox intentional atonement, not works framing.

**Minor doctrinal-rhetorical stretch (not heresy, but accuracy-adjacent):**

> "would **both** divide his clothes and gamble for them"  
> "**Both**, to the letter."

Psalm 22:18 is Hebrew **synonymous parallelism** — “part my garments” / “cast lots upon my vesture” is classically one scene stated twice, not two independent predictions. The ledger admits this (“verse’s own parallelism”) but the **spoken** line still sells two foreseen acts. The two-step literal split (four parts + separate lot for the seamless coat) comes from **John’s narrative** (19:23–24), not self-evidently from David’s single poetic line.

That is an overclaim for a script whose brief is “accuracy #1” and whose metadata says the convergence must be “honest.” Standard evangelical reading? Yes. Honest as spoken? Not fully.

---

### Clarity on first hearing (zero Bible knowledge)

**Hook — strong.**  
> "A thousand years before a Roman cross, a king wrote a song describing a death he would never die."

Paradox + time gap lands in ~5 seconds. Grips.

**David pronouns — mostly fixed, one stumble.**  
> "He was describing someone else: stripped, surrounded, his life poured out."

On audio, “He” immediately after “David… he died an old man” can momentarily sound like David again before the colon clarifies. Not fatal, but not crystal.

**Proof — the biggest clarity gap.** A listener with no Bible background hears:

1. Old song says killers divide clothes and gamble for them.
2. At Jesus’ cross, soldiers divide clothes and gamble.
3. “Both, to the letter.”

What they do **not** hear: *why this instance is prophecy and not routine crucifixion procedure.* Roman soldiers commonly divided victims’ clothing. The airtight part of the case — John **names Psalm 22 as fulfilled** — is buried inside a paraphrase. The quoted John phrase `"that the scripture might be fulfilled"` is never explained as “John said this happened **because the psalm said it would**.” Without that bridge, the proof can sound like: soldiers did what soldiers always do → therefore miracle.

The script also never signals John 19:23’s **seamless coat** specificity — the detail that makes “split clothes, then dice for the rest” more than generic looting. That omission weakens first-hearing persuasion for the very audience this format targets.

**“John watched it at the cross”** — Who is John? Was he there? John 19 narrates soldier actions; it does not establish John as eyewitness to the dice throw. Zero-knowledge listener may not know John from Judas.

Knowledge and invitation, not threat or transaction. “For you” personalizes grace; it does not slide into gain-loss (“do this or lose”). No works framing.

Caveat: **“Both, to the letter”** oversells the psalm’s poetic structure and recycles a “precision prophecy” trope without earning it for a naive listener (see clarity section).

### Summary judgment

v4 decisively fixes the convergent pierced problem and is doctrinally sound. It is **not lock-ready** under accuracy-first standards because: (1) the “both / two acts” framing overclaims what Psalm 22:18 alone predicts, (2) first-hearing proof does not bridge “soldiers gambled for clothes” → “John says Psalm 22 was fulfilled,” and (3) the John quote carries non-KJV terminal punctuation.

VERDICT: REVISE
TOP FIXES:
1. Reframe the proof climax so “both, to the letter” credits **John’s account** (four parts + lot for the seamless coat / explicit fulfillment citation), not David’s single parallel line as two separate predictions — e.g. drop “both” from the pre-quote setup or say the psalm’s line “landed as two literal acts at the cross.”
2. Add one bridging phrase so a zero-Bible listener hears **why** this isn’t generic soldier behavior — e.g. that John records it and says *that the scripture might be fulfilled* — naming that he’s citing **this psalm**, not just narrating routine crucifixion.
3. Fix the John 19:24 quotation form (comma, not period; or extend through `which saith`) and trim 5–10 words elsewhere if runtime is already at ~66s.
