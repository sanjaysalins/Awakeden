The header claims "the SHORT does NOT rest on that one word" and that v3 demoted it, but the spoken text does not reflect that demotion in placement or emphasis.

**Spotlighting of the disputed "pierced" detail persists (core v2 failure not resolved):** After establishing the garments as primary with the explicit John citation ("soldiers split Jesus' clothes, then gamble for the last piece — 'that the scripture might be fulfilled.' Two precise acts, written centuries early."), the script immediately continues:

"The same psalm even says:

**[narrator — KJV, Psalm 22:16]**

'…they pierced my hands and my feet.'

Long before the nails, God had written the rescue down — so you'd know the cross was no accident."

"The same psalm even says" + the pierced quote + the very next sentence's "the nails" (anaphora directly referencing the just-quoted hands/feet) puts the contested detail in the strongest rhetorical position at the end of the proof section. This is the opposite of demotion. The header's "Catalog episode: 'The Crucifixion Foretold' <Psalm 22:16>" and "Beat distilled: the predictive precision — pierced hands/feet (22:16) + garments..." still foreground 22:16 in the framing.

**"Romans perfected crucifixion" is imprecise and rhetorically loaded:** Exact opening hook: "Almost a thousand years before the Romans perfected crucifixion, a king wrote a song describing a death he would never die."

"Perfected" implies Rome refined or elevated the method in a notable way. Historical crucifixion (impalement/crucifixion forms) predates Rome by centuries (Persians, Carthaginians, etc.). The surrounding depth note acknowledges this ("avoids over-claiming Rome invented it") but the spoken hook still uses the loaded verb for effect.

**"with your name in it" creates first-hearing clarity risk and a mild self-contradictory name-word collision:** 

"Long before the nails, God had written the rescue down — so you'd know the cross was no accident. It was the plan, with your name in it.

The suffering man of Psalm twenty-two has a name: Jesus."

For a listener with zero Bible knowledge, "your name in it" (an ancient psalm/plan literally containing the listener's name) is not obvious or transparent. It reads as poetic conceit rather than clear meaning ("this was written so you would recognize the cross as the planned event for you"). The immediate follow-up "has a name: Jesus" creates two different "name" referents in adjacent sentences with no disambiguation. This is the exact sort of "clever-but-confusing conceit" the lens requires flagging.

**"the rescue" appears without prior textual setup:** The line "God had written the rescue down" is the first mention of rescue/vindication. Psalm 22 as presented up to that point is only "a song describing a death," "stripped, surrounded, his life poured out," and clothes being divided. The turn to "rescue" + personal application therefore feels like an unearned theological insertion rather than something the listener can follow from the details given.

**Landing is thin and does not clearly do "NEW work":** "The suffering man of Psalm twenty-two has a name: Jesus. He went to that cross on purpose — for you. Come to Him."

The "on purpose" does echo the written-plan thread. But "Come to Him" is a minimal, standard invitation that does not leverage the specific two-acts/garments/John convergence or the "David died old in his own bed" contrast. It is functionally close to the generic closes the spec says to avoid.

**Minor clarity / first-hearing friction in the David setup:** "Psalm twenty-two — David, in the first person, describing a man stripped, surrounded, his life poured out. But David died old, in his own bed."

"Watch one detail — he wrote that the man's killers would divide his clothes."

The rapid "David, in the first person, describing a man" followed by "he" (David) writing about "the man" is logically sound once parsed, but on first audio pass it requires the listener to track the distinction without visual scaffolding. "a king" in the hook is not named until the next line.

**"Long before the nails" adds a detail not present in the quoted psalm text:** The psalm quote says "pierced," not "nails." Specifying "nails" + placing it immediately after the pierced quote reinforces the very reading the v3 header claims to have de-emphasized.

**"Two precise acts" is an improvement over the v2 "not this many,"** and the garments + John attachment is now correctly foregrounded in the spoken proof. The "David died old, in his own bed" fix helps zero-Bible clarity. The grace-anchored tone ("for you. Come to Him.") avoids fear/gain-loss framing. The single thread (prophetic details written in advance identify the man as Jesus and show the cross was the plan for you) holds without swapping. No invented doctrine is asserted beyond standard evangelical predictive/typological reading of Psalm 22.

The v3 changes are real on paper and in the depth notes, but the spoken narration and metadata have not fully executed the claimed restructure. The disputed "pierced" element remains the last and most visually/emotionally salient proof line.
