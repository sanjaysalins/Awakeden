## Independent adversarial review — *The Crucifixion Foretold* (Psalm 22 short #1)

### KJV verbatim — PASS (with one framing caveat)

Checked against project KJV cache:

- `"…they pierced my hands and my feet."` — exact clause from Psalm 22:16 KJV.
- `"They part my garments among them, and cast lots upon my vesture."` — exact Psalm 22:18.
- `"the scripture might be fulfilled"` — exact substring from John 19:24 KJV.

No altered words inside quotation marks. The leading ellipsis on 22:16 is an excerpt marker, not a text change.

**Caveat (not a KJV violation, but accuracy-adjacent):** John 19:24’s fulfilled citation is *garments/vesture only* — `"They parted my raiment among them, and for my vesture they did cast lots."` It does **not** cite `"they pierced my hands and my feet."` The script’s narrator line bundles both details under John’s witness.

---

### Doctrinal soundness — mostly PASS, one overreach

Orthodox evangelical Messianic reading of Psalm 22; no contrarian text-critical hot take in the spoken lines; no invented doctrine.

**Overreach:** `"Those pierced hands were planned"` and `"Jesus — pierced for you"` move from *predictive prophecy* into *substitutionary atonement* without a beat of support in this 60s cut. Orthodox, but it widens the claim beyond what the proof block actually establishes. A careful listener could hear “John proved the pierced-hands prophecy” — he didn’t.

---

### Convergence honesty — FAIL (biggest problem)

The brief and DEPTH section say proof rests on **undisputed convergence (garments + John’s explicit citation)**, not the disputed *pierced* reading. The spoken script **does not honor that**.

1. **Proof order still leads with the disputed line.** First quoted detail is `"…they pierced my hands and my feet."` Garments — the detail John actually names — come second.

2. **John attribution overclaims.**  
   `"and John says it happened so 'the scripture might be fulfilled.'"`  
   John documents soldiers casting lots for clothes so **garment** scripture would be fulfilled. He does not attach that phrase to pierced hands/feet. On first hearing, this sounds like John vouching for *both* quoted lines. That is imprecise in a script whose #1 priority is accuracy.

3. **“Not this many” is mathematically thin.** Only **two** details are spoken. `"One detail could be coincidence. Not this many"` reads like a stack of convergences; a skeptical listener gets exactly one undisputed NT-backed hit (garments) plus one textually contested hit (pierced). The long-form’s honest move — concede *pierced*, let convergence carry it — is **not** compressed here; it’s reversed.

4. **Conviction doubles down on the disputed element.** `"Those pierced hands were planned"` directly contradicts the stated design intent (“weight OFF the disputed ‘pierced’ word”). The red-team fix did not fully land in the spoken text.

---

### Clarity on first hearing — REVISE

**Hook vs. immediate correction — self-contradiction risk**

- Hook: `"a man described his own execution — in the first person."`
- Next beat: `"David, writing as though it were his own body"`

A zero-Bible-knowledge listener hears “David described his own crucifixion,” then “as though” — which sounds like a walk-back. David did not die stripped on a cross. The long-form handles this explicitly (“David himself never came near a death like the one he describes”); the short does not.

**Unexplained leap to Jesus**

`"Psalm twenty-two has a name written all the way through it. Jesus"` — What name? Written where? This is a clever line with no on-ramp. A new listener has heard David → soldiers → John → “not coincidence” and is suddenly told the psalm “has a name… Jesus” with no bridge (Messiah, first-person voice not David’s death, Jesus quoting Psalm 22 on the cross, etc.). Logic gap, not a thread swap — but clarity cost is real.

**“Rescue” without object**

`"God wrote the rescue down"` — rescue *from what*, *for whom*? Grace-leaning, but vague on first pass.

No fear, gain-loss, works, or “do better” framing. `"you were on His mind"` and `"Come to Him"` invite rather than pressure. Spirit convicts; script invites. Clean on this lens.

### Freshness = faithful depth — mixed

**Fresh:** thousand-year gap, Roman crucifixion form, gambling for clothes at the cross — concrete, text-grounded surprise.

**Stale / overclaimed:** `"the cross was no accident"` and `"a thousand years on schedule"` are strong tags but partly undercut by the John/convergence imprecision above. Freshness here is supposed to be *honest* convergence; the garments beat is genuinely striking — it doesn’t need the pierced line carrying equal rhetorical weight.

Single thread: ancient first-person execution poem → specific details → cross fulfillment → divine intent → Jesus → invitation. No thread swap.

Minor wobble: landing adds `"pierced for you"` (atonement) where the spine to that point was *foreknowledge / proof*, not *personal substitution* — extension, not a new thread, but slightly widened.
