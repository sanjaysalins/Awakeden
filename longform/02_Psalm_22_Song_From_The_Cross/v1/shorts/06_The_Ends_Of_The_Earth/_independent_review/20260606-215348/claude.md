## The historical over-read — the one real watch-point

The guard you set says the safe claim is "peoples FROM the nations," not whole-nation conversion. But the *spoken* phrase "nation after nation turned to God" can land on a zero-Bible listener as **corporate / whole-nation conversion** — i.e., entire nations turning, which is historically and doctrinally stronger than "people from every nation." The ledger correctly clarifies "people FROM the nations," but that clarification is not audible. This is the only place the short leans past its own guard. If you want zero exposure, "from nation after nation, people turned to God" closes it without losing the rhythm.

## Person-conflation — acceptable, sharpest point named
"nation after nation turned to **God**, worshipping the very One who had died" identifies the crucified Christ as the object of the nations' worship. The psalm itself has the nations worshipping Yahweh ("before thee" = God), not explicitly the sufferer. The short performs the NT identification (Yahweh-worship → worship of the crucified-risen Lord) **without claiming the psalm names Jesus** — it says "turned to God" then attributes the worship to Christ via the gospel reading. That's the orthodox move (Phil 2:10-11, Rev 5). No conflation flag. It is the doctrinally loadest line, so I'm naming it, but it holds.
