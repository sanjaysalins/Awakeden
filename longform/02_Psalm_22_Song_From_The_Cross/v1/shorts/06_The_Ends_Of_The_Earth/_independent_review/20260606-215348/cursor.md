## Independent adversarial review — Psalm 22 short #6 (“The Ends of the Earth”)

### KJV verbatim — PASS (quoted span only)

The single quoted line matches project KJV cache exactly:

> "All the ends of the world shall remember and turn unto the LORD: and all the kindreds of the nations shall worship before thee."

No altered words inside quotation marks. Non-quoted paraphrases ("turn to God") are outside quotes and acceptable.

---

### Doctrinal soundness — mostly sound; one compression risk

**Resurrection hinge dropped.** The long-form locks the arc as forsaken → **death-to-life turn (vv. 22–24)** → nations (v. 27). This short jumps:

> "After the suffering, the song throws its arms open to every nation:"

…straight into v. 27. On a beat about *accuracy*, that skips the psalm’s own internal turn from forsaken corpse-voice to living worshipper. A listener can hear nations turning as the direct fruit of **suffering/death alone**, not the psalm’s death-**and**-vindication structure. Not heresy, but a real compression of what the psalm actually does between agony and v. 27.

**Worship object named by death only.**

> "worshipping the very One who had died"

Orthodox intent is clear (brief: crucified-risen Christ). Spoken text stops at death. Worship of the crucified Lord in NT terms is worship of the **risen** Lord (Rev 5; Phil 2:10–11). Death-only wording is a small Christological truncation, not full person-conflation — but it’s a residual slip on an accuracy-first beat.

**"Turn unto the LORD" → Christ — acceptable, with listener risk.** Identifying Yahweh-turning with worship of the One who died on the cross is standard evangelical typology and does **not** claim the psalm names Jesus. Fine if the series has already established Jesus ↔ psalm. Cold listeners may not track *who* "the very One who had died" is without that prior beat.

No invented doctrine, no contrarian readings, no universalism in the landing offer.

---

### Accuracy guard — main residual issue: over-realized fulfillment

The brief’s yellow guard is prophecy (v. 27) + **historical** gospel spread, not completed eschatological fulfillment. Spoken text mostly honors that with:

> "nation after nation turned to God"

…but then undercuts it with:

> "It sounded impossible. **Then it happened.**"

The quoted verse is future/prophetic — "**shall** remember and turn" / "**shall** worship." The narration correctly keeps that future in:

> "his song says the ends of the earth **will** turn to God"

Then **"Then it happened"** + past tense **"turned"** collapses "shall" into a finished historical fact. That is the guard’s core worry: sounding like v. 27 is **done**, not **inaugurated** (Great Commission spread) with eschatological remainder (Rev 7:9 multitude still future).

**Internal metadata drift (not spoken, but signals design slippage):** beat line says *"the gospel did go to **every nation**"* while the guard says *"nation after nation" not "literally every nation."* Spoken text follows the guard; the header does not.

---

### Clarity on first hearing — good for series; two friction points

**Hook (series #6):** Strong for returning viewers:

> "The psalm Jesus cried from the cross begins in agony — one man, forsaken, dying. But look how it ends: with the whole world."

Jesus is named immediately; arc is clear. Cold audience without Psalm 22 context is weaker but acceptable for a numbered series beat.

**Identity drop after the quote:** Line 1 = Jesus. Post-quote:

> "**A man** dying in one corner of the Roman Empire"

No name. Series context probably carries it; a zero-Bible listener could momentarily lose *which* dying man without re-anchoring.

**Chronology slack:**

> "From **that cross** the message went out"

The gospel went out post-resurrection/Pentecost, not from the cross moment itself. Evangelical shorthand, but on an accuracy-first script it’s imprecise enough to flag.

### Grace-anchored conviction — PASS

No fear, gain/loss, works, or self-interest framing. Conviction is carried by the scale of the psalm’s arc; the close is invitation:

> "Come to Him."

Spirit convicts; script invites. Clean.

### Freshness = faithful depth — PASS (with one soft note)

Faithful surprises: one forsaken man → ends of the earth; impossible prophecy → historical spread; global scope narrowed to *you*. No clickbait readings.

Soft note: *"throws its arms open to every nation"* anthropomorphizes the song — vivid, not confusing, slightly poetic for a KJV-heavy beat.

---

### One thread spine — PASS

Single thread holds: forsaken solitary agony → worldwide turn (v. 27) → gospel spread → personal reach → "Come to Him." No thread-swapping.

This does specific work: geographic prophecy → present listener. *"Come to Him"* alone is generic, but paired with *"reaching all the way to you"* it echoes v. 27 and is not a tired trust-close. Acceptable for 60s.

VERDICT: REVISE
TOP FIXES:
1. Replace **"Then it happened"** / past-perfect fulfillment tone with inaugurated language (e.g. "And it began" / "nation after nation **has** turned" / "the turning started") so v. 27's **"shall"** is not heard as fully accomplished history.
2. Insert a one-beat resurrection/turn bridge before v. 27 — or change **"After the suffering"** to something that reflects the psalm's death-to-life turn, not suffering alone.
3. Change **"worshipping the very One who had died"** to **"who died and rose"** (or "crucified and risen") and soften **"From that cross the message went out"** to death-and-resurrection/Pentecost wording.
