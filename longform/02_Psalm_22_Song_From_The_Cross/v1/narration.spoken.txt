At the ninth hour, nailed to a Roman cross and unable to fill his lungs, Jesus of Nazareth gathered his strength and cried out into the dark. "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" We hear raw anguish — a man forsaken. And it is that. But it is also a quotation. Those exact words had been written down, in the first person, almost a thousand years before that morning — by a king who died old and safe in his own bed. "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me? why art thou so far from helping me, and from the words of my roaring?" This is Psalm twenty-two. And the dying Jesus did not reach for it by accident. He was telling everyone within earshot exactly where to look. King David wrote this song. He wrote it in the first person — I, me, my — as if it were happening to his own body. But here is the strangeness: David himself never came near a death like the one he describes. Because what he describes is a public execution. A man stripped, surrounded, and mocked to death. "But I am a worm, and no man; a reproach of men, and despised of the people. All they that see me laugh me to scorn: they shoot out the lip, they shake the head…" And listen to what the mockers actually say: "He trusted on the LORD that he would deliver him: let him deliver him, seeing he delighted in him." David was a soldier and a king who died of old age — never stripped before a jeering crowd, never executed at all. So whose death is he describing — in his own voice — with such certainty that he writes it down as a fact? Then the song turns forensic. It stops sounding like poetry and starts sounding like a witness statement from a cross that would not be invented for centuries. "I am poured out like water, and all my bones are out of joint…" The body of a man hung up by his arms — the joints pulling apart under his own weight. "…my tongue cleaveth to my jaws; and thou hast brought me into the dust of death." A killing thirst — on the cross, the apostle John records the cry: I thirst. Then the song says the thing it has no business knowing this early: "…they pierced my hands and my feet." Pierced hands and feet. David lived a thousand years before Rome turned crucifixion into a science; his own people never executed that way. There was no reason on earth for him to picture a man fixed to wood through his hands and feet — yet there it is, in ink, centuries early. And it keeps going, down to the smallest detail: "They part my garments among them, and cast lots upon my vesture." Soldiers gambling for a dead man's clothes. Turn to the foot of the cross, and John tells you it happened: "…that the scripture might be fulfilled, which saith, They parted my raiment among them, and for my vesture they did cast lots." The Roman soldiers gambling for his clothes were not following a script. They were fulfilling one anyway. Now — an honest objection, and it deserves a straight answer, not a sales pitch. A careful reader will say: this is poetry. David is a man who reached for vivid images — dogs, a lion — when his enemies pressed him. And on that one word, pierced, there is a genuine scholarly dispute: the traditional Hebrew text can be read as like a lion at my hands and feet rather than they pierced. That is a real question, honestly raised. So concede it cleanly. Take pierced off the table entirely, just for a moment. You are still left with a man stripped and mocked to death, his bones out of joint, dying of thirst, his clothing gambled away by the people who killed him — written first-person, by a king who suffered none of it. One disputed word does not carry that. The convergence does. And on the word itself: the Greek Old Testament — translated by Jewish scholars almost two centuries before Jesus was born — already read it they dug, or pierced. This is not a Christian edit smuggled in after the fact. The reading was there, in Jewish hands, centuries before there was a cross to defend. David is describing a death that is not his own. Which brings us back to the cross, and to why that screamed cry matters so much. When a first-century Jew quoted the opening line of a psalm, he was rarely reaching for just one line. The most natural way to hear it was as a pointer to the whole song — the first verse pulling the entire psalm into the room. So when Jesus cried — "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" — he was not merely voicing his pain. He was placing himself as the I of Psalm twenty-two. Every line. He was claiming the wounded hands as his own, and the gambled clothes as his own — even as the soldiers below him divided them up, exactly as verse eighteen said they would. He took the whole song onto himself, out loud, in front of witnesses. He was telling us: this one is about me. But most people stop reading Psalm twenty-two at the cross. And if you stop there, you miss the most important thing about it. The song does not end in a grave. Halfway through, it turns — violently, gloriously — from death to life. The same voice that cried "forsaken" suddenly stands up in the middle of a worshipping crowd: "I will declare thy name unto my brethren: in the midst of the congregation will I praise thee." A dead man declares nothing. Yet the voice that cried "forsaken" now stands alive in the congregation, praising. You could read that as mere rescue from the edge of death — except that Jesus took this whole song onto himself, and the New Testament puts this very line, "I will declare thy name unto my brethren," in the mouth of the risen Christ. The forsaken man of verse one and the living worshipper are the same person, on the far side of death — because God did not leave him in the dust: "…neither hath he hid his face from him; but when he cried unto him, he heard." And then the song throws its arms open to the entire world: "All the ends of the world shall remember and turn unto the LORD: and all the kindreds of the nations shall worship before thee." The psalm that opens with one forsaken man closes with the nations streaming home. The cross was never the end of the song. It was the hinge. Look at the very last line David writes. After the wounds, after the turn, the song closes on a note of something accomplished — a work finished: "…he hath done this." A thousand years later, on the cross, the dying Jesus reaches the same note. He receives the vinegar, and says: "It is finished." Done. He hath done this. So hold the two ends of the song together. The cry was real — on the cross he truly bore the forsaking that our sin had earned, in our place. And yet the same psalm tells you how it ends: God did not hide his face, he heard, he raised him up. The forsaking fell on him so that it would never fall on you. And the wounded hands were spread wide enough to reach the ends of the earth — every kindred, every nation, everyone far off. That includes you. You do not have to make yourself worth saving before you come. That is the whole point of Psalm twenty-two: it ends not with what you must do, but with what he hath done — and the congregation it ends in still has room for you. The song has been reaching toward you the whole time. Come and join the ones who praise him.
