## Current draft — the 7 movements (spoken text; **bold KJV** = verbatim quote, must not change)

**M1 · THE REPORT**
Seven hundred years before a Roman nail was ever driven through a human wrist, a prophet in Jerusalem wrote down the death of a man he had never met. He described the wounds, the silence, the grave — and the rich man who would own it. He even wrote down what the wounds were *for*. And then he asked the question that still hangs over the page: **"Who hath believed our report? and to whom is the arm of the LORD revealed?"** (Isa 53:1) This is Isaiah fifty-three. Either it is the most astonishing accident in all of human literature — or someone standing outside of time was telling us, centuries early, exactly how God would save the world.

**M2 · BEHOLD MY SERVANT**
It opens with God Himself pointing. **"Behold, my servant shall deal prudently, he shall be exalted and extolled, and be very high."** (Isa 52:13) You brace yourself for a conquering king. And then the picture breaks. **"his visage was so marred more than any man, and his form more than the sons of men"** (Isa 52:14) — a face beaten past recognising. **"he hath no form nor comeliness; and when we shall see him, there is no beauty that we should desire him. He is despised and rejected of men; a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief."** (Isa 53:2-3) God's chosen Servant does not arrive in armour. He arrives in agony. The world looks at him — and looks away. "We hid as it were our faces from him." The exalted one and the despised one are the same man. Hold those two together — that tension is the whole gospel in a single breath.

**M3 · THE EXCHANGE**
Then comes the centre of the chapter — and every line is a transaction. **"Surely he hath borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows"** (Isa 53:4) — *our* griefs. Not his own. **"But he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed."** (Isa 53:5) Stay on the small words, because they carry the whole weight. *Our* transgressions. *Our* iniquities. *Our* peace. *His* wounds. This is not a good man dying as a brave example. This is a substitution: one stands in the place of many and takes what they had earned. And in case you think you are watching this from a safe distance, Isaiah reaches out and pulls you in: **"All we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned every one to his own way; and the LORD hath laid on him the iniquity of us all."** (Isa 53:6) All of us. Every one. The straying is named — and then the rescue: laid on him.

**M4 · THE SILENT LAMB & THE HONEST QUESTION**
Then watch *how* he suffers. **"He was oppressed, and he was afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth: he is brought as a lamb to the slaughter, and as a sheep before her shearers is dumb, so he openeth not his mouth."** (Isa 53:7) No defence. No curse on his accusers. Silent. Now — an honest question, because this would not be honest without it. For centuries many thoughtful Jewish readers have understood this Servant not as one man, but as the nation of Israel itself — a suffering people, despised among the nations. That is a serious reading, and it deserves a serious answer, not a sneer. Here is the answer the text gives about itself. Look again: **"for the transgression of my people was he stricken."** (Isa 53:8) The Servant is stricken *for* "my people" — so the Servant cannot simply *be* the people. He stands in their place. The sufferer and the ones he suffers for are two, not one. And the details refuse to fit a nation. **"he made his grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death; because he had done no violence, neither was any deceit in his mouth."** (Isa 53:9) A single grave. A rich man's tomb. A whole life with no violence and no deceit in it — sinless. No nation in history has ever been honestly described that way. This is not a people. This is a person.

**M5 · OF WHOM SPEAKETH THE PROPHET THIS?**
Which leaves the only question that matters. *Who is he?* The New Testament records the exact moment someone asked it out loud. A man from Ethiopia — a court official — is riding home in his chariot, reading this very chapter aloud: **"He was led as a sheep to the slaughter; and like a lamb dumb before his shearer, so opened he not his mouth."** (Acts 8:32) Philip, sent by the Spirit, comes alongside the chariot. And the Ethiopian turns and asks the question Isaiah seems to invite: **"I pray thee, of whom speaketh the prophet this? of himself, or of some other man?"** (Acts 8:34) And Luke gives the answer in one unforgettable line. **"Then Philip... began at the same scripture, and preached unto him Jesus."** (Acts 8:35) He did not have to wrestle the text into shape. He started at Isaiah fifty-three, and the road led straight to Jesus of Nazareth. The report Isaiah wondered if anyone would believe — the New Testament answers: *this is the man.*

**M6 · IT PLEASED THE LORD**
But there is one line in this chapter heavier than all the wounds. **"Yet it pleased the LORD to bruise him; he hath put him to grief."** (Isa 53:10) *Pleased.* Not because the Father delights in pain — but because He delights in what the cross would accomplish: a people brought home. And the Servant is no victim caught in the gears — Isaiah says he "poured out his soul unto death." He *gave* it. Then, quietly, the chapter turns toward morning. **"he shall see his seed, he shall prolong his days... He shall see of the travail of his soul, and shall be satisfied."** (Isa 53:10-11) A man bruised to death does not live to see his days prolonged — unless death is not the end of him. Buried in verse nine; alive again in verse eleven. The resurrection is hiding in plain sight, written centuries before the empty tomb was ever found. And looking back over all of it, the risen Servant feels one word: *satisfied.* It was enough. It worked.

**M7 · THE ARM OF THE LORD**  *(conviction + landing)*
So come back to where Isaiah began. **"Who hath believed our report? and to whom is the arm of the LORD revealed?"** (Isa 53:1) The arm of the LORD — the saving strength of God — is not a thing. It is a person. It has a face, marred more than any man's. It has hands, pierced for transgressions that were never his own. And it has your name in verse six. The straying it names — "we have turned every one to his own way" — is yours, and mine. But read where the sentence ends: "the LORD hath laid on him the iniquity of us all." Every way you turned that was your own — laid on him. Already carried. Already paid in full. You do not have to clean yourself up before you come. That is the whole point of Isaiah fifty-three: he was wounded so that you would not have to be. The report is true. The arm of the LORD has a name. His name is Jesus. Have you believed the report?
