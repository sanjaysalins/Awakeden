[slow] Seven hundred years before a Roman nail was ever driven through a human wrist, a prophet in Jerusalem wrote down the death of a man he had never met.

[deliberate] He described the wounds, the silence, the grave — and the rich man who would own it. He even wrote down what the wounds were for.

[slow] And then he asked the question that still hangs over the page:

<speaker name="narrator">"Who hath believed our report? and to whom is the arm of the LORD revealed?"</speaker>

[reflective] This is Isaiah fifty-three. Either it is the most astonishing accident in all of human literature — or someone standing outside of time was telling us, centuries early, exactly how God would save the world.

[serious] It opens with God Himself pointing.

<speaker name="the_LORD">"Behold, my servant shall deal prudently, he shall be exalted and extolled, and be very high."</speaker>

[deliberate] You brace yourself for a conquering king. And then the picture breaks.

<speaker name="narrator">"his visage was so marred more than any man, and his form more than the sons of men"</speaker>

A face beaten past recognising.

<speaker name="narrator">"he hath no form nor comeliness; and when we shall see him, there is no beauty that we should desire him. He is despised and rejected of men; a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief."</speaker>

[reflective] God's chosen Servant does not arrive in armour. He arrives in agony. The world looks at him — and looks away.

[slow] The exalted one and the despised one are the same man. Hold those two together — that tension is the whole gospel in a single breath.

[deliberate] Then comes the centre of the chapter — and every line is a transaction.

<speaker name="narrator">"Surely he hath borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows"</speaker>

Our griefs. Not his own.

<speaker name="narrator">"But he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed."</speaker>

[deliberate] Stay on the small words. Our transgressions. Our peace. His wounds.

This is not a good man dying as a brave example. This is a substitution: one stands in the place of many and takes what they had earned.

[serious] And in case you think you are watching this from a safe distance, Isaiah reaches out and pulls you in:

<speaker name="narrator">"All we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned every one to his own way; and the LORD hath laid on him the iniquity of us all."</speaker>

[reflective] All of us. Every one. The straying is named — and then the rescue: laid on him.

[slow] Then watch how he suffers.

<speaker name="narrator">"He was oppressed, and he was afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth: he is brought as a lamb to the slaughter, and as a sheep before her shearers is dumb, so he openeth not his mouth."</speaker>

[deliberate] No defence. No curse on his accusers. Silent.

Now — an honest question. For centuries many thoughtful Jewish readers have understood this Servant not as one man, but as the nation of Israel itself — a suffering people, despised among the nations.

And they have a real text behind them. Earlier in this same book, God calls the nation by this very title:

<speaker name="narrator">"Thou art my servant, O Israel."</speaker>

[deliberate] So reading chapter fifty-three as Israel is not a dodge. It is the natural first guess. It deserves a serious answer, not a sneer.

Here is what the chapter does that no nation can survive. First, the sufferer is sinless:

<speaker name="narrator">"he made his grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death; because he had done no violence, neither was any deceit in his mouth."</speaker>

No people in history — not a nation, not even its most faithful remnant — has ever been honestly described that way.

Second, he dies for the people:

<speaker name="narrator">"for the transgression of my people was he stricken."</speaker>

[reflective] The Servant stands in their place — so he cannot simply be them. A single grave. A rich man's tomb. A sinless life. This is not a people. This is a person.

[slow] Which leaves the only question that matters. Who is he?

The New Testament records the exact moment someone asked it out loud. A man from Ethiopia — a court official — is riding home in his chariot, reading this very chapter aloud:

<speaker name="narrator">"He was led as a sheep to the slaughter; and like a lamb dumb before his shearer, so opened he not his mouth."</speaker>

[deliberate] Philip, sent by the Spirit, comes alongside the chariot. And the Ethiopian turns and asks the question Isaiah seems to invite:

<speaker name="eunuch">"I pray thee, of whom speaketh the prophet this? of himself, or of some other man?"</speaker>

[slow] And Luke gives the answer in one unforgettable line.

<speaker name="narrator">"Then Philip... began at the same scripture, and preached unto him Jesus."</speaker>

[reflective] He did not have to wrestle the text into shape. He started at Isaiah fifty-three, and the road led straight to Jesus of Nazareth. The report Isaiah wondered if anyone would believe — the New Testament answers: this is the man.

[slow] But there is one line in this chapter heavier than all the wounds.

<speaker name="narrator">"Yet it pleased the LORD to bruise him; he hath put him to grief."</speaker>

[deliberate] Pleased. Not because the Father delights in pain — but because He delights in what the cross would accomplish: a people brought home. And the Servant is no victim caught in the gears — Isaiah says he poured out his soul unto death. He gave it.

Then, quietly, the chapter turns toward morning.

<speaker name="narrator">"he shall see his seed, he shall prolong his days... He shall see of the travail of his soul, and shall be satisfied."</speaker>

[reflective] A man bruised to death does not live to see his days prolonged — unless death is not the end of him. Buried in verse nine; alive in verse eleven. Isaiah does not spell out an empty tomb — but he leaves a shape that only resurrection fills, a shadow the New Testament would bring into full light.

And looking back over all of it, the risen Servant feels one word: satisfied. It was enough. It worked.

[slow] So come back to where Isaiah began.

<speaker name="narrator">"Who hath believed our report? and to whom is the arm of the LORD revealed?"</speaker>

[reflective] The arm of the LORD — the saving strength of God — is not a thing. It is a person. It has a face, marred more than any man's. It has hands, pierced for transgressions that were never his own.

[deliberate] And it has your name in verse six. The straying it names — "we have turned every one to his own way" — is yours, and mine. But read where the sentence ends: "the LORD hath laid on him the iniquity of us all." Every way you turned that was your own — laid on him. Already carried. Already paid in full.

[slow] You do not have to clean yourself up before you come. That is the whole point of Isaiah fifty-three: he was wounded so that you would not have to be.

The report is true. The arm of the LORD has a name. His name is Jesus.

[reflective] Have you believed the report?
