# Independent review — codex (OK, 90s)

Findings:

- KJV verbatim issue: **"Then Philip... began at the same scripture, and preached unto him Jesus."** is labeled KJV, Acts 8:35, but the KJV reads: “Then Philip opened his mouth, and began at the same scripture, and preached unto him Jesus.” The ellipsis makes this not verbatim.

- KJV verbatim issue: **"he shall see his seed, he shall prolong his days... He shall see of the travail of his soul, and shall be satisfied."** is labeled KJV, Isaiah 53:10-11, but it stitches two verses with an ellipsis and omits substantial KJV wording. If the rule is verbatim quotation, this needs to be split or quoted fully.

- Doctrinal overstatement: **"he was wounded so that you would not have to be."** That is too broad and potentially misleading. Isaiah’s claim is not that believers avoid wounds or suffering, but that the Servant bears sin, guilt, chastisement, and brings healing/peace.

- Doctrinal precision issue: **"The arm of the LORD — the saving strength of God — is not a thing. It is a person."** This is rhetorically strong but theologically compressed. In Isaiah, “arm of the LORD” is a metaphor for God’s revealed saving power; saying it “is” a person may overstate the image. Safer: the arm of the LORD is revealed in the Servant, fulfilled in Christ.

- Hook overclaim: **"the rich man who would own it"** goes beyond Isaiah’s wording. Isaiah says **"with the rich in his death"**; the ownership detail comes from Gospel fulfillment, not directly from Isaiah 53. This risks making the prophecy sound more mechanically specific than the text itself.

- Invented interiority: **"the risen Servant feels one word: satisfied."** Isaiah says **"shall be satisfied"**, but “feels one word” psychologizes the Servant beyond the text. Keep the claim anchored: God says the Servant will see the travail of his soul and be satisfied.

- Clarity risk: **"Either it is the most astonishing accident in all of human literature — or someone standing outside of time..."** is gripping, but it frames the reading as a forced apologetic binary. For long-form teaching, this may feel more like pressure than exposition.

- Strong section: the Israel objection is handled respectfully and textually. **"So reading chapter fifty-three as Israel is not a dodge"** is fair, and the answer from **"he had done no violence"** plus **"for the transgression of my people was he stricken"** is one of the script’s clearest moments.

VERDICT: REVISE
TOP FIXES:
1. Fix all non-verbatim KJV quotations, especially Acts 8:35 and Isaiah 53:10-11.
2. Replace “he was wounded so that you would not have to be” with a sin-bearing/forgiveness/healing claim faithful to Isaiah 53.
3. Tighten overstatements: “arm of the LORD is a person,” “rich man who would own it,” and “the risen Servant feels one word.”
