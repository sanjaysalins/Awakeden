## Movement 1 — The Picture

Here is one of the strangest cures in the Bible. A whole camp is dying of snakebite — and God's remedy is not to kill the snakes, or heal the wounds, or hand out an antidote. He tells them to look at a piece of metal on a pole. Look, and live.

It happens near the end of the wilderness years. Israel is worn down by the long road, and the old bitterness boils over. **"Wherefore have ye brought us up out of Egypt to die in the wilderness? for there is no bread, neither is there any water; and our soul loatheth this light bread."** They have bread from heaven — and they despise it, and accuse the God who has carried them this far.

And then the ground itself turns against them. **"And the LORD sent fiery serpents among the people, and they bit the people; and much people of Israel died."**

## Movement 2 — The Problem

Picture the camp. The venom is already moving, and there is nothing a bitten man can do to stop it — he cannot run from a poison inside his own blood.

And notice what the people ask for. They come to Moses and confess: **"We have sinned… pray unto the LORD, that he take away the serpents from us."** Take away the serpents — fix the surroundings, make the threat go away. But that would not save a single person already bitten. The danger outside was never the deepest problem; the poison inside was. And that is the human problem in miniature: we beg God to change our circumstances, when the thing that is killing us is already under our own skin.

## Movement 3 — The Strange Detail

Now watch what God actually does — because He does not do what they asked.

He does not take the serpents away. He says to Moses: **"Make thee a fiery serpent, and set it upon a pole: and it shall come to pass, that every one that is bitten, when he looketh upon it, shall live."**

Sit with how strange that is. The cure is shaped like the curse. The thing lifted up is a serpent — the very image of what is killing them — raised on a pole for all to see. God does not clear the serpents from the camp; He lifts up the image of the judgment itself, and turns the dying eye toward it.

And the cure costs the bitten man nothing he can boast in — not a potion to brew, not a wound to lance, not a snake to fight. **"Every one that is bitten, when he looketh upon it, shall live."** One act. Look.

**"And Moses made a serpent of brass, and put it upon a pole, and it came to pass, that if a serpent had bitten any man, when he beheld the serpent of brass, he lived."**

## Movement 4 — The Centuries-Early Match

Now move forward roughly fourteen hundred years, into a quiet night-time conversation. A religious leader named Nicodemus has come to Jesus in the dark, trying to understand who He is. And Jesus reaches back — past the temple, past the law — to a snake on a pole in the desert.

**"And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up: That whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have eternal life."**

Stop on that. This is not a preacher centuries later finding a clever picture in the Old Testament. This is Jesus Himself, naming that pole as a portrait of His own cross.

And He means it precisely. Later, John tells us what that phrase pointed to: **"And I, if I be lifted up from the earth, will draw all men unto me."** John adds the note: **"This he said, signifying what death he should die."** "Lifted up" was the cross. The wilderness pole was always pointing at Calvary.

And the passage flows straight on into the verse the whole world knows: **"For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son…"** The snake on the pole and "God so loved the world" sit in the same breath of Scripture.

## Movement 5 — The Honest Objection

Now, be fair to the doubts, because there are two.

First: doesn't a bronze snake on a pole just become an idol? Scripture itself answers that — bluntly. Centuries later Israel did begin burning incense to it, and a godly king named Hezekiah **"brake in pieces the brasen serpent that Moses had made."** The bronze never had power; God did. Looking was never magic — it was trust, aimed where God told them to aim it. Scripture smashes the relic itself, so that you never mistake the sign for the Savior.

Second, and deeper: isn't a *serpent* a strange picture of Christ? In the Bible the serpent is the tempter and the curse. Exactly — and that is the point, not the problem. What hung on that pole was the likeness of the very thing killing them; what hung on the cross was the One of whom Scripture says God **"hath made him to be sin for us, who knew no sin"** — the One **"made a curse for us: for it is written, Cursed is every one that hangeth on a tree."** Christ did not become a sinner. He was lifted up bearing our curse, in the likeness of the judgment we deserved — so the look of faith could find it there and live. And this is not a stretch read into the text; it is the meaning Jesus put there Himself.

## Movement 6 — The Exchange

So hold the two scenes together. In the wilderness, the cure was never *in* the dying man — he could not neutralize the venom or earn his way back to health. It hung entirely outside him, on a pole, and became his the moment he looked.

That is the cross. We are all bitten; the poison of sin is already in us, and no self-improvement reaches it. So God lifted up His Son, who **"his own self bare our sins in his own body on the tree."** The curse fell on the lifted One, so that it would pass by everyone who looks to Him.

It cost the bitten Israelite nothing but a turn of the eyes. It cost the Son everything.

## Movement 7 — The Invitation

There is one more detail here, easy to miss, and it may be the kindest of all: the cure was a *look*.

God could have asked for a climb, a payment, a proof of strength. He asked for the one thing a dying man can still do. You can be too weak to stand and still lift your eyes. The most poisoned person in the camp was no further from healing than the least — both were saved the same way, by looking away from themselves to the One lifted up. Faith, in the end, is not a great work you produce; it is the empty-handed look of someone who has stopped trying to cure themselves.

So the invitation is as wide as the word Jesus chose: **"whosoever."** You do not have to get the poison out first, or feel strong, or be certain your faith is large enough. You only have to stop staring at the bite, and look up.

The serpent in the wilderness was God teaching the world one motion that saves: look, and live. He lifted His Son up on a cross for exactly that — so that *whosoever* turns their eyes to Him **"should not perish, but have eternal life."** Look to Him, and live.
