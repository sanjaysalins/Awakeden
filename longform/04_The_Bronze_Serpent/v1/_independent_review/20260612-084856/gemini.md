Here are the critical findings based on your requested lenses:

**1. Thread-Swapping / Broken Momentum (Movement 5)**
*   *The text:* "First: a bronze snake on a pole sounds like a magic charm, or an idol. Doesn't this teach people to worship an object? ... a godly king named Hezekiah brake in pieces the brasen serpent..."
*   *The critique:* This violently breaks the "one thread spine" rule. You just brought the listener to Jesus and John 3:16 in M4, hitting the emotional peak of the script. Then, in M5, you slam on the brakes, yank the listener 700 years backward into Old Testament history, and start playing defense against a niche theological hypothetical about idolatry. A zero-knowledge listener is *not* worried about whether this story violates the second commandment; they are trying to process the cross. This detour kills the momentum and introduces unnecessary complexity.

**2. Doctrinal Imprecision / Confused Metaphor (Movement 5)**
*   *The text:* "Christ did not become a sinner. He took the curse — He was lifted up *as* our poison..."
*   *The critique:* This violates the "clarity on first hearing" and "doctrinal soundness" lenses. In Movement 2, you establish that the "poison" is the internal corruption of sin ("already under our own skin"). But Christ did not become our internal corruption; He bore our penalty and our *curse*. Furthermore, the bronze serpent on the pole notably *lacked venom*—that was the whole point of it being a bronze likeness (matching Romans 8:3, "in the likeness of sinful flesh"). Calling Jesus "our poison" is theologically jarring and breaks your own internal metaphor. Keep it to "lifted up bearing our curse."

**3. Missed Opportunity to Close the Loop (Movement 2 to Movement 7)**
*   *The text (M2):* "...we keep asking God to change our circumstances, when the thing that is killing us is already under our own skin."
*   *The text (M7):* "You only have to stop staring at the bite, and look up."
*   *The critique:* Movement 2 introduces a brilliant, grace-anchored psychological insight: we want God to fix our external circumstances when the real threat is internal. But the landing in M7 doesn't fully capitalize on this setup to do "new work." To make the CTA truly airtight, M7 should explicitly remind the listener of M2's premise: faith is the moment you stop demanding God fix your circumstances, stop trying to extract the poison yourself, and simply look to the One who took the curse for you.

VERDICT: REVISE
TOP FIXES:

3. Sharpen the CTA in Movement 7 by tying it explicitly back to the profound insight in Movement 2—connect the act of "looking up" to the cessation of trying to fix our own circumstances.
