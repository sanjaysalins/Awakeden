# The Bronze Serpent — long-form narration (DRAFT v1)

**Series:** types-shadows (#2) · **Format:** 16:9, ~6–8 min, 7-movement spine
**Core text:** Numbers 21:4–9 · **NT fulfillment:** John 3:14–15 (Jesus' own words) · John 12:32–33 · 2 Cor 5:21 · Gal 3:13 · 1 Pet 2:24

## Movement 1 — The Picture

Here is one of the strangest cures in the Bible. A whole camp is dying of snakebite — and God's remedy is not to kill the snakes, or heal the wounds, or hand out an antidote. He tells them to look at a piece of metal on a pole. Look, and live.

It happens near the end of the wilderness years. Israel is worn down by the long way around Edom, and the old bitterness boils over. **"Wherefore have ye brought us up out of Egypt to die in the wilderness? for there is no bread, neither is there any water; and our soul loatheth this light bread."** They have bread from heaven, and they despise it. They have come this far, and they accuse the God who carried them.

And then the ground itself turns against them. **"And the LORD sent fiery serpents among the people, and they bit the people; and much people of Israel died."**

## Movement 2 — The Problem

Picture the camp now. The bite is in them. The venom is already moving, and there is nothing a bitten man can do to stop it — he cannot run from a poison that is inside his own blood.

And notice what the people ask for. They come to Moses and confess: **"We have sinned, for we have spoken against the LORD, and against thee; pray unto the LORD, that he take away the serpents from us."** Take away the serpents. Fix the surroundings. Make the threat go away.

But that would not save a single person already bitten. The danger outside was never the deepest problem. The poison inside was. And that is the human problem in miniature — we keep asking God to change our circumstances, when the thing that is killing us is already under our own skin.

## Movement 3 — The Strange Detail

Now watch what God actually does — because He does not do what they asked.

He does not take the serpents away. He says to Moses: **"Make thee a fiery serpent, and set it upon a pole: and it shall come to pass, that every one that is bitten, when he looketh upon it, shall live."**

Sit with how strange that is. The cure is shaped like the curse. The thing lifted up is a serpent — the very image of what is killing them — raised on a pole for all to see. God does not remove the poison from the camp; He lifts up a picture of it, and turns the dying eye toward it.

And the cure costs the bitten man nothing he can boast in. Not a potion to brew, not a wound to lance, not a snake to fight. **"Every one that is bitten, when he looketh upon it, shall live."** One act. Look. The strongest soldier and the weakest child are healed exactly the same way — by turning their eyes to the pole.

**"And Moses made a serpent of brass, and put it upon a pole, and it came to pass, that if a serpent had bitten any man, when he beheld the serpent of brass, he lived."**

## Movement 4 — The Centuries-Early Match

Now move forward roughly fourteen hundred years, into a quiet night-time conversation. A religious leader named Nicodemus has come to Jesus in the dark, trying to understand who He is. And Jesus reaches back — past the temple, past the law — to a snake on a pole in the desert.

**"And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up: That whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have eternal life."**

Stop on that. This is not a preacher centuries later finding a clever picture in the Old Testament. This is Jesus Himself, naming that pole as a portrait of His own cross. *As Moses lifted up the serpent... even so must the Son of man be lifted up.*

And He means it precisely. Later, John tells us what that phrase pointed to: **"And I, if I be lifted up from the earth, will draw all men unto me."** John adds the note: **"This he said, signifying what death he should die."** "Lifted up" was the cross. The wilderness pole was always pointing at Calvary.

And the very next sentence Jesus speaks is the one the whole world knows: **"For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son…"** The snake on the pole and "God so loved the world" are the same breath.

## Movement 5 — The Honest Objection

Now, be fair to the doubts, because there are two good ones.

First: a bronze snake on a pole sounds like a magic charm, or an idol. Doesn't this teach people to worship an object? Scripture itself answers that — and bluntly. Centuries later, Israel kept the bronze serpent and started burning incense to it, and a godly king named Hezekiah **"brake in pieces the brasen serpent that Moses had made… and he called it Nehushtan"** — a name that dismissed it as nothing but a piece of bronze. The metal never had power. God did. Looking was never magic; it was trust, aimed where God told them to aim it. The Bible smashes the idol itself, to make sure you never mistake the sign for the Savior.

Second, and deeper: isn't a *serpent* a strange picture of Christ? In the Bible the serpent is the tempter, the curse, the enemy. Exactly — and that is the point, not the problem. What hung on that pole was the likeness of the very thing killing them. And what hung on the cross was the One of whom Scripture says God **"hath made him to be sin for us, who knew no sin"** — the One **"made a curse for us: for it is written, Cursed is every one that hangeth on a tree."** Christ did not become a sinner. He took the curse — He was lifted up *as* our poison, so the look of faith could find it there and live. That is not a stretch read into the text. It is the meaning Jesus put there Himself.

## Movement 6 — The Exchange

So hold the two scenes together.

In the wilderness, the cure was never *in* the dying man. He could not neutralize the venom or earn his way back to health. The cure was entirely outside him, lifted up on a pole, and it became his the moment he looked.

That is the cross. We are all bitten — the poison of sin is already in us, and no amount of self-improvement reaches it. So God lifted up His Son, who **"his own self bare our sins in his own body on the tree."** The judgment we had coming fell on Him. The curse landed on the lifted One, so that it would pass by the ones who look to Him.

It cost the bitten Israelite nothing but a turn of the eyes. It cost the Son everything.

## Movement 7 — The Invitation

There is one more thing in this story that is easy to miss, and it may be the kindest detail of all. The cure was a *look*.

God could have asked for a climb, a payment, a pilgrimage, a proof of strength. He asked for the one thing a dying man can still do. You can be too weak to stand and still lift your eyes. You can be the most poisoned person in the camp and be no further from healing than the least — because both of you are saved the same way, by looking away from yourselves to the One lifted up. Faith, in the end, is not a great work you produce. It is the empty-handed look of someone who has stopped trying to cure themselves.

So this is the invitation, and it is as wide as the word Jesus chose: **"whosoever."** You do not have to get the poison out first. You do not have to feel strong, or be certain your faith is large enough. You only have to stop staring at the bite, and look up.

The serpent in the wilderness was God teaching the world one motion that saves: look, and live. He lifted His Son up on a cross for exactly that — so that *whosoever* turns their eyes to Him **"should not perish, but have eternal life."** Look to Him, and live.
