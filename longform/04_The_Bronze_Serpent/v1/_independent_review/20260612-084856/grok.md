"He was lifted up *as* our poison, so the look of faith could find it there and live." (Movement 5). The script has already defined "poison" and "venom" as the literal content moving in the blood ("the venom is already moving, and there is nothing a bitten man can do to stop it"; "the poison of sin is already in us"). Equating the crucified Christ with "our poison" after that usage is a category error. The two sentences that follow ("Christ did not become a sinner. He took the curse") do not erase the sentence that precedes them for a listener hearing it once. The controlling verses already quoted ("hath made him to be sin for us, who knew no sin"; "made a curse for us: for it is written, Cursed is every one that hangeth on a tree") are stronger and sufficient. The "poison" line is loose and unnecessary.

"The cure is shaped like the curse. The thing lifted up is a serpent — the very image of what is killing them — raised on a pole for all to see." (Movement 3). This typological identification is asserted while still in the Numbers narrative. The listener must then sit through the entire Nicodemus scene ("Now move forward roughly fourteen hundred years") before reaching Movement 5's defensive question ("isn't a serpent a strange picture of Christ?") and the two verses that actually carry the weight. For first-hearing clarity the striking claim cannot travel for several minutes without its guardrails.

"and he called it Nehushtan" — a name that dismissed it as nothing but a piece of bronze. (Movement 5). The quoted words from 2 Kings 18:4 stop at the name. The verse does not supply the meaning or the dismissive paraphrase. The gloss is extra-textual explanation presented as the point of Hezekiah's action. The header claims "KJV quotes verbatim" and notes the gloss was deliberately softened from a harder etymology; the current version still adds content the verse withholds.

"God does not remove the poison from the camp; He lifts up a picture of it, and turns the dying eye toward it." (Movement 3). "Poison" is again used for what the bronze serpent actually images (the serpent itself as the form of the judgment). The repeated slippage between venom, the biting serpent, the bronze representation, and then Christ "as our poison" creates cumulative imprecision.

The thread itself ("God does not remove the serpents; He tells Moses to lift one up") is consistent from the opening negation through Movement 7 and does not swap. The landing re-uses the story's own language ("look, and live"; "whosoever... should not perish, but have eternal life") rather than defaulting to the most generic close.
