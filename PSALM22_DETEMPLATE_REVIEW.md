# Psalm 22 shorts — DE-TEMPLATED hooks + CTAs (for red-team + panel review)

These 8 gospel shorts originally ALL closed on 'Come to Him' and 6/8 opened 'a thousand years before the cross'. They were de-templated so no two share a hook or CTA. KJV quotes + the doctrinal middle are UNCHANGED. Judge: (1) does each NEW hook grip in ~5s and stay true to its verse? (2) is each NEW CTA grace-anchored (no fear/gain-loss/pressure), landing on JESUS, NOT tired/cheesy/soft, doctrinally sound? (3) did de-templating break the thread or flow? (4) as a SET, is the variety genuine (no new shared template)? KJV is already verified verbatim.

## 01_The_Crucifixion_Foretold
- NEW HOOK: Ten centuries before the first nail, a song already knew how the dying man's clothes would be divided.
- NEW CTA : He was stripped so you could be clothed in Him — turn to Him.
- FULL spoken: Ten centuries before the first nail, a song already knew how the dying man's clothes would be divided.
Psalm twenty-two. David wrote it in the first person — but David himself was never executed; he died an old man. He was describing someone else: stripped, surrounded, his life poured out.
Watch one line — he wrote that the dying man's killers would divide his clothes, and cast lots for them:
"They part my garments among them, and cast lots upon my vesture."
A thousand years later, John watched it at the cross — soldiers dividing Jesus' clothes, then casting lots for the seamless coat — "that the scripture might be fulfilled".
A thousand years early, God had it written down — so when it happened, you'd know the cross was no accident. It was the plan.
The suffering man of Psalm twenty-two has a name: Jesus, promised a thousand years before He came. He was stripped so you could be clothed in Him — turn to Him.

## 02_The_Mockers_Words
- NEW HOOK: The taunts they would scream at the cross were written down, word for word, a millennium early.
- NEW CTA : Let it in.
- FULL spoken: The taunts they would scream at the cross were written down, word for word, a millennium early.
Psalm twenty-two. David, describing a mocked and dying man, even writes his tormentors' words:
"…they shake the head, saying, He trusted on the LORD that he would deliver him: let him deliver him…"
A thousand years later, at the cross, Matthew records both — the passers-by wagging their heads, and the religious leaders jeering:
"He trusted in God; let him deliver him now…"
The same gesture. The same taunt — flung at Him by people who never saw what they were echoing.
"Let him deliver him," they sneered. And He wasn't delivered — not because Jesus lacked the power to come down; He had every power. He stayed because staying was the only way to deliver you.
They thought weakness pinned Him to that cross. It was love they couldn't measure, and it is still reaching for you. Let it in.

## 03_The_Forsaken_Cry
- NEW HOOK: The loneliest sentence Jesus ever spoke wasn't His own line — He was quoting.
- NEW CTA : He went into that dark to bring you home — so run home to the Father who waited.
- FULL spoken: The loneliest sentence Jesus ever spoke wasn't His own line — He was quoting.
Psalm twenty-two opens in the voice of a forsaken man. David wrote it; Jesus made it His own.
David's very first line:
"My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"
A thousand years later, at the ninth hour, Jesus cried it from the cross:
"My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"
The very same words.
This was no cry of lost faith — even forsaken, He still calls God "my God." It was the sinless Son taking our place, and bearing the forsaking that our sin deserved.
He was forsaken so that you never will be. He went into that dark to bring you home — so run home to the Father who waited.

## 04_Declared_To_The_Brethren
- NEW HOOK: Everyone stops reading Psalm twenty-two at the cross.
- NEW CTA : Step into it.
- FULL spoken: Everyone stops reading Psalm twenty-two at the cross. Keep going — it doesn't end in a grave.
But the cry doesn't stay forsaken. Later, the psalm turns — the same voice moves from anguish to praise:
"I will declare thy name unto my brethren: in the midst of the congregation will I praise thee."
Who is that praising voice? Hebrews takes that opening line — word for word — and puts it in the mouth of the risen Christ. The psalm's turn is Jesus, alive on the far side of the cross.
And hear what He calls us. Hebrews says He is —
"not ashamed to call them brethren"
— brothers. Family.
The same Jesus who cried out forsaken now lives, and the risen Christ is not ashamed to call you family. It ends not in a grave, but a family. Step into it.

## 05_He_Hath_Done_This
- NEW HOOK: A psalm's last line and Jesus' last breath in John both close on the same note: finished.
- NEW CTA : There's nothing left for you to finish; rest in the One who said it is done.
- FULL spoken: A psalm's last line and Jesus' last breath in John both close on the same note: finished.
The psalm closes looking ahead — to God's saving work, accomplished, and told to a people not yet born. Its final line:
"…that he hath done this."
Done. And as Jesus hung dying, John records His final word:
"It is finished".
Different words, in different tongues, a thousand years apart — but the same note: a finished work.
So what, exactly, was finished? Everything it would take to bring you home to God — nothing left for you to earn, to prove, or to add. There's nothing left for you to finish; rest in the One who said it is done.

## 06_The_Ends_Of_The_Earth
- NEW HOOK: It opens with one forsaken man dying alone.
- NEW CTA : Answer it.
- FULL spoken: It opens with one forsaken man dying alone. It ends with every nation on earth.
After the suffering, the song throws its arms open to every nation:
"All the ends of the world shall remember and turn unto the LORD: and all the kindreds of the nations shall worship before thee."
A man dying in one corner of the Roman Empire — and his song says the ends of the earth will turn to God. It sounded impossible. But from that cross, and the empty tomb, the gospel went out — and people in nation after nation have turned to the LORD, worshipping the One who died and rose.
That is the reach of the cross. It was never a local tragedy — it was for the nations, all the kindreds of the earth.
And "the ends of the world" includes wherever you are, right now. That song was reaching all the way to you. Answer it.

## 07_The_Body_Foretold
- NEW HOOK: A king described a body coming apart so precisely it reads like an autopsy of a crucifixion.
- NEW CTA : Bring Him your broken places.
- FULL spoken: A king described a body coming apart so precisely it reads like an autopsy of a crucifixion.
Psalm twenty-two. David wrote it in the first person, but he was describing someone else's death, not his own. Watch the body of the dying man:
"I am poured out like water, and all my bones are out of joint…"
Drained — and every joint pulled loose, the way a body hangs when it's suspended by the arms. And:
"I may tell all my bones: they look and stare upon me."
He could count every bone, stretched and exposed, while onlookers stood and stared at him.
David never saw a crucifixion. Rome would not make it its instrument for centuries. Yet the dying man of his song bears the marks of one — written early.
This is what Jesus bore to bring you home — every wrenched joint, every cold stare, endured willingly, for you. Bring Him your broken places.

## 08_I_Thirst
- NEW HOOK: The One who made every ocean gasped two words on a cross: I thirst.
- NEW CTA : The fountain ran dry for you; come and drink.
- FULL spoken: The One who made every ocean gasped two words on a cross: I thirst.
Psalm twenty-two. David wrote it in the first person, but he was picturing someone else's death. Watch the body give out:
"My strength is dried up like a potsherd; and my tongue cleaveth to my jaws; and thou hast brought me into the dust of death."
Dried clay; a tongue stuck fast; a man sinking into the dust of death. Then John records the dying Jesus' cry:
"I thirst."
Psalm twenty-two doesn't foretell that cry — it paints the thirst behind it, a thousand years before He felt it.
And think who is thirsting: the One who made every river and spring, who offers the world living water — hanging there with nothing, in your place.
He went dry so that you could drink the living water and never thirst again. The fountain ran dry for you; come and drink.
