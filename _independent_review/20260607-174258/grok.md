**Remaining problems (adversarial review):**

**#01_The_Crucifixion_Foretold**  
CTA and closing line: "blind to the trade being made — His life, laid down to buy you back."  
The full script repeats the same commercial framing in the final paragraph. "Trade being made" and "buy you back" introduce marketplace/transactional imagery for the atonement. This is not required by the Psalm 22 clothes detail or the John 19 fulfillment. It risks shifting from substitution to a deal struck for the listener's benefit. Under the grace-anchored and doctrinal soundness lenses, this is the clearest new problem introduced in the fix round. "Blind to the trade" is also a clever conceit that a zero-knowledge listener must decode in real time.

**#03_The_Forsaken_Cry**  
CTA: "However far you've run, the way home is open — and He opened it from the dark."  
The preceding sentence: "He was forsaken so that you never will be."  
"However far you've run" assumes the listener already has a prior relationship with God and has wandered. This violates the clarity criterion for a listener with zero Bible knowledge. The line does not follow for someone hearing the gospel for the first time; it sounds addressed to religious people who already know the "home" story. The substitution itself ("forsaken so that you never will be") is sound; the second-person narrative assumption is not.

**#04_Declared_To_The_Brethren**  
Narration: "Who is that praising voice? Hebrews takes that opening line — word for word — and puts it in the mouth of the risen Christ."  
The quoted line is Psalm 22:22 ("I will declare thy name unto my brethren..."), not the opening line of the psalm (which is the forsaken cry). The phrasing is imprecise and risks misleading the listener about what Hebrews actually quotes.  
CTA: "There's room in that word for you."  
This is rhetorical word-play on "brethren." It does not do substantive new work pointing to Christ's person, finished work, or the cross/resurrection. It lands as a preacher's clever observation rather than a grace-anchored invitation. Borderline cheesy under the stated criteria.

**#07_The_Body_Foretold**  
CTA: "He was broken in your place."  
The narration builds with specific, vivid details ("I am poured out like water, and all my bones are out of joint…", "I may tell all my bones: they look and stare upon me."). The landing is a generic substitution phrase that does not do fresh work tied to those details or the "plan" thread. After the eyewitness-style prophecy, it feels abrupt and thin. "Broken in your place" is doctrinally acceptable but does not meet the "landing does NEW work" or non-cheesy standard the set is being held to.

**#08_I_Thirst**  
CTA: "And that water is a Person."  
The script correctly notes that Psalm 22 "doesn't foretell that cry" (the "I thirst" of John 19:28) but "paints the thirst behind it." It then pivots to Jesus offering "living water" and "the one water that never runs dry." This imports the living-water motif (John 4/7) as the payoff for a Psalm 22 thirst description. The move is interpretive layering rather than tight exposition of the text under discussion.  
The CTA itself is another short epigram/slogan ("that water is a Person"). Combined with "He could hand you the one water," it has the same rhetorical-closer feel as #04. Not the worst doctrinal problem, but it fails the "not cheesy" and "new work" tests on a confirmation round.

**#06_The_Ends_Of_The_Earth** (minor)  
CTA line: "the Lord it promised still has room..."  
The narration frames Psalm 22 as describing one man dying alone and then the global response. The psalm itself is not presented as "promising the Lord"; the sufferer in the psalm is the Lord who suffers. The phrasing is slightly loose.

**#02_The_Mockers_Words**  
Now clean on the prior near-heresy. "With you in view" is personal but acceptable in context. No new doctrinal break.

**#05_He_Hath_Done_This**  
Clean. "Nothing — only Someone..." does the required work.
