# Independent review — grok (OK, 84s)

The CTA in #05 ("So if He has already said it is done, what are you still trying to finish?") directly interrogates the listener's current activity. The sentence before it ("nothing left to earn, to prove, to add") states finished work, but the question assumes the hearer is "still trying to finish" something and demands an implicit account of why. For a first-time listener this registers as a rebuke about their ongoing effort rather than a rest in what Christ declared done. The final clause does not name or point to Christ; it points at the listener's supposed striving.

#02 CTA ("He stayed, for you.") and the line immediately before it ("They jeered that He couldn't save Himself, and they were right about one thing: He wouldn't.") mix threads. The short's core quotation and title are the mockers' words from Psalm 22:8 ("He trusted on the LORD that he would deliver him: let him deliver him"). The "couldn't save Himself" language is the separate taunt from Matthew 27:42 / Mark 15:31. The narration attributes the "couldn't" jeer to the same "they" in the flow of the Psalm 22 taunt, then flips it to "He wouldn't." This is not a clean reversal inside the 22:8 thread; it imports a different cross mockery about self-rescue power and presents it as part of the psalm-recorded sneer.

#06 CTA ("That song began with one man dying alone and kept widening until it reached you; you are the ends of the earth it was singing toward.") makes the individual viewer the telos of the psalm. The quoted text says the ends of the world "shall remember and turn unto the LORD" and worship. The CTA reframes the ancient song as having sung toward "you" personally. This is an over-personalization of a corporate prophetic statement about the nations. The final clause lands on the viewer's inclusion ("you are...") rather than on Christ or the worship the psalm actually declares.

#04 CTA ("The risen Christ looked at the world that nailed Him down and called it brethren — and there's room in that word for you.") and the prior sentence overstate the referent. Hebrews 2 applies "my brethren" and "not ashamed to call them brethren" to those who are sanctified and the children God gave the Son, in the context of the congregation. "The world that nailed Him down" is the hostile kosmos in the gospel accounts; the text does not have the risen Christ calling that world "brethren." The claim blurs the biblical boundary of who is addressed as family.

#07 CTA ("He came apart to hold you together.") uses a compressed paradox drawn from the body details ("poured out like water," "all my bones are out of joint," "I may tell all my bones"). On first hearing the phrase "came apart to hold you together" is unclear without the preceding description and leans toward therapeutic language (personal coherence or integration) rather than the atonement categories the rest of the series uses (bringing home, delivering, opening the way). The purpose clause does not specify what "hold you together" means biblically.

#01 CTA ("They rolled dice for His last belongings, never seeing He was giving away something far greater — Himself, for you.") stays at the level of ironic exchange ("giving away ... for you") without stating what the giving achieved. "Giving away something far greater" is abstract for a zero-Bible listener. The landing is still "for you" as beneficiary without further doctrinal payload in the closing sentence itself.

#03 CTA ("However far you've run, the way home is open — and He opened it from the dark.") opens the final sentence with an assumption about the listener ("However far you've run"). This sets up the solution by first positing the hearer's distance as a given. The second half ("He opened it from the dark") does point to Christ's action, but the structure still centers the listener's prior "run" before the Christological claim.

#08 CTA ("The well that never runs dry is a Person.") names a Person as the answer, which satisfies the "lands on Christ" test better than several others. However, the spoken lead-in imports "living water" and "the thirst in you could finally be answered" (John 4/7 themes) immediately before the CTA. The short's middle and Psalm 22 quotation stay inside physical dryness and thirst; the spiritual-thirst frame appears only at the close. The metaphor "well that never runs dry" is also a stock Christian phrase rather than fresh depth derived from this psalm's text.

Across the set, the syntactic variety (question, paradox, short declarative, viewer-reframe) is present on the page, but the final movement in most cases still resolves to direct second-person address centering the listener as the one "for" whom or "toward" whom the action occurred ("for you," "for you," "you never will be," "for you," "you still trying," "reached you; you are...," "hold you together," "thirst in you"). This creates a recurring register even while the grammar changes.

VERDICT: REVISE
TOP FIXES:
1. Replace the #05 CTA question ("what are you still trying to finish?") with a declarative sentence that rests on the finished work and unmistakably points to Christ and what He accomplished, removing the direct interrogation of the listener's supposed ongoing effort.
2. Correct the #04 claim: change "The risen Christ looked at the world that nailed Him down and called it brethren" to language that matches Hebrews' actual scope (the sanctified, the congregation He is not ashamed to call brethren) without attributing the title to the hostile world as a whole.
3. Revise the #06 CTA so the final clause does not identify the individual viewer as "the ends of the earth it was singing toward"; keep the application inside the psalm's corporate claim about the nations turning to the LORD or re-center the close on Christ rather than the listener's personal inclusion as the song's target. Also align #02's "couldn't save Himself" line strictly to the Psalm 22:8 taunt thread or clearly mark it as a separate mockery.
